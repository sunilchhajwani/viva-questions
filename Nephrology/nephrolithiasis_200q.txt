**Q21:** You are choosing between IV Ketorolac and IV Morphine for this patient's pain. What did the REVERSE trial conclude about these options?

**A21:** The REVERSE trial (and others) demonstrated that NSAIDs are more effective than opioids for renal colic pain, providing better relief and reducing the need for rescue analgesia.

**Q22:** Why are NSAIDs particularly effective in the pathophysiology of renal colic?

**A22:** NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandin synthesis, which reduces ureteral smooth muscle contraction and decreases renal blood flow/intrapelvic pressure, addressing the root cause of the pain.

**Q23:** The patient has a history of mild renal insufficiency. How does this affect your choice of Ketorolac?

**A23:** Acute renal insufficiency or chronic kidney disease are relative contraindications for NSAIDs due to the risk of further impairing renal perfusion; cautious use or alternatives (opioids) may be necessary.

**Q24:** If the REVERSE trial favors NSAIDs, under what clinical circumstance would you still prioritize an opioid?

**A24:** Opioids are prioritized when NSAIDs are contraindicated (e.g., severe renal failure, history of GI bleed, or late-stage pregnancy) or when rapid-onset 'bridge' analgesia is needed.

**A62:** The diagnosis is a 'septic stone' or obstructive pyelonephritis, which is a surgical emergency requiring urgent decompression to prevent urosepsis and renal loss.

**Q63:** What laboratory findings would further support the severity of this infection?

**Q64:** Why is the combination of obstruction and infection so dangerous in the urinary tract?
