# Master Case: Airway Management & Procedures

## Section 1: Rapid Sequence Intubation (RSI)

**Q1: What are the 7 P's of RSI?**
A: Preparation, Preoxygenation, Pretreatment, Paralysis with Induction, Protection & Positioning, Placement with Proof, Post-intubation Management.

**Q2: What is the standard dose of succinylcholine for adult RSI?**
A: 1.5 mg/kg IV (rapid onset 45-60 seconds, duration 5-10 minutes).

**Q3: When is succinylcholine contraindicated?**
A: Known hyperkalemia, burns >24hrs old, crush injuries >24hrs, neuromuscular diseases (MS, ALS, Guillain-Barre), stroke >72hrs, pseudocholinesterase deficiency.

**Q4: What is the preferred induction agent in hemodynamically unstable patients?**
A: Etomidate (0.3 mg/kg) - minimal cardiovascular effects.

**Q5: What dose of rocuronium is used for RSI?**
A: 1.0-1.2 mg/kg for rapid onset (60 seconds).

**Q6: How do you reverse rocuronium in a cannot-intubate-cannot-oxygenate (CICO) scenario?**
A: Sugammadex 16 mg/kg (high dose for immediate reversal).

**Q7: What is the BURP maneuver?**
A: Backward, Upward, Rightward Pressure on the thyroid cartilage to improve glottic view.

**Q8: Define difficult airway.**
A: Difficulty with face mask ventilation, difficulty with tracheal intubation, or difficulty with supraglottic airway placement.

**Q9: What is the LEMON law for airway assessment?**
A: Look externally, Evaluate 3-3-2 rule, Mallampati score, Obstruction, Neck mobility.

**Q10: What does the 3-3-2 rule assess?**
A: 3 fingerbreadths mouth opening, 3 fingerbreadths hyoid-mental distance, 2 fingerbreadths thyroid-to-mouth floor distance.

## Section 2: Difficult Airway Algorithms

**Q11: What is the first step in a failed intubation algorithm?**
A: Call for help, optimize position (ramping), ensure adequate preoxygenation, attempt with video laryngoscope.

**Q12: When should you proceed to cricothyrotomy?**
A: After failed intubation attempts AND failed bag-mask ventilation (CICO situation).

**Q13: What size endotracheal tube for adult males/females?**
A: Males: 7.5-8.0 mm, Females: 7.0-7.5 mm.

**Q14: How do you confirm endotracheal tube placement?**
A: Direct visualization, ETCO2 waveform (gold standard), bilateral breath sounds, chest rise, fogging in tube, CXR.

**Q15: What is the most reliable method to confirm ET tube placement?**
A: Continuous waveform capnography (ETCO2).

**Q16: What is the SELICK maneuver?**
A: Cricoid pressure during RSI to prevent aspiration (controversial, may worsen view).

**Q17: What is the optimal head position for intubation?**
A: Sniffing position (ear aligned with sternal notch), or ramped position in obese patients.

**Q18: What video laryngoscope blade types exist?**
A: Hyperangulated (Glidescope, C-MAC D-blade) and standard geometry (Glidescope Miller, C-MAC standard).

**Q19: When would you use a bougie?**
A: Anticipated difficult airway, poor glottic view (Cormack-Lehane III/IV), as first-line adjunct.

**Q20: How do you use a bougie correctly?**
A: Advance through vocal cords, feel for "clicks" on tracheal rings, hold-up sign at carina (~40cm), railroad ET tube over it.

## Section 3: Procedural Sedation

**Q21: What are the levels of sedation?**
A: Minimal (anxiolysis), Moderate (conscious sedation), Deep sedation, General anesthesia.

**Q22: What monitoring is required for procedural sedation?**
A: Continuous pulse oximetry, ECG, BP q5min, ETCO2 for deep sedation, dedicated observer.

**Q23: What is the dose of ketamine for procedural sedation?**
A: 1-2 mg/kg IV (onset 1 min, duration 10-20 min) or 4-5 mg/kg IM.

**Q24: What are ketamine's advantages?**
A: Preserves airway reflexes, bronchodilation, analgesia, maintains sympathetic tone.

**Q25: What are ketamine's contraindications?**
A: Increased ICP, uncontrolled hypertension, ischemic heart disease, psychosis, thyrotoxicosis.

**Q26: What is the dose of propofol for sedation?**
A: 0.5-1 mg/kg bolus, then 25-75 mcg/kg/min infusion.

**Q27: What are propofol's risks?**
A: Hypotension, respiratory depression, apnea, pain on injection, propofol infusion syndrome (prolonged use).

**Q28: What is the dose of midazolam for sedation?**
A: 0.05-0.1 mg/kg IV (max 5mg), onset 2-3 min.

**Q29: How do you reverse benzodiazepines?**
A: Flumazenil 0.2 mg IV, repeat q1min up to 1mg total (caution: seizure risk in chronic users).

**Q30: What is dissociative sedation?**
A: State where patient appears awake but is disconnected from environment (ketamine effect).

## Section 4: Central Venous Access

**Q31: What are the common central line sites?**
A: Internal jugular, subclavian, femoral vein.

**Q32: Which site has lowest infection risk?**
A: Subclavian (but highest pneumothorax risk).

**Q33: Which site has highest infection risk?**
A: Femoral vein.

**Q34: What ultrasound findings confirm venous vs arterial?**
A: Vein: non-pulsatile, compressible, thin-walled. Artery: pulsatile, non-compressible, thick-walled.

**Q35: What is the Seldinger technique?**
A: Needle puncture → guidewire insertion → needle removal → dilator over wire → catheter over wire → wire removal.

**Q36: How do you confirm central line placement?**
A: Aspiration of blood, CXR (tip at SVC-RA junction), ultrasound, transduction of waveform.

**Q37: What complication presents with sudden hypotension during subclavian attempt?**
A: Tension pneumothorax.

**Q38: What is the Trendelenburg position used for?**
A: IJ/subclavian access to increase venous filling and reduce air embolism risk.

**Q39: How do you manage an accidental arterial puncture during central line placement?**
A: Remove needle, apply firm pressure 10+ min, avoid cannulation, monitor for hematoma.

**Q40: What length catheter for right IJ?**
A: 15-20 cm; Left IJ: 20-25 cm; Femoral: 20-25 cm.

## Section 5: Chest Procedures

**Q41: What are indications for needle decompression?**
A: Tension pneumothorax with hemodynamic instability.

**Q42: Where do you perform needle decompression?**
A: 2nd intercostal space midclavicular line OR 4th/5th ICS anterior axillary line (preferred, longer catheters).

**Q43: What size catheter for needle decompression?**
A: Minimum 8 cm (14G or larger), traditional 5cm often inadequate.

**Q44: Where is the safe triangle for chest tube insertion?**
A: Bounded by: lateral edge of pectoralis major, lateral edge of latissimus dorsi, 5th ICS/nipple line, apex at axilla.

**Q45: What size chest tube for pneumothorax?**
A: 16-22 French (smaller acceptable for primary spontaneous).

**Q46: What size chest tube for hemothorax?**
A: 28-36 French (larger to prevent clotting).

**Q47: How do you orient chest tube holes?**
A: All holes must be inside pleural space, last hole ~2cm inside chest wall.

**Q48: What is the proper direction for chest tube insertion?**
A: Pneumothorax: apical/posterior. Hemothorax: basal/posterior.

**Q49: What complication causes subcutaneous emphysema after chest tube?**
A: Tube malposition (holes outside pleural space), inadequate drainage.

**Q50: When can you remove a chest tube?**
A: Lung fully expanded, no air leak 12-24hrs, minimal drainage (<150-200ml/day).

## Section 6: Cardiac Procedures

**Q51: What are indications for transcutaneous pacing?**
A: Symptomatic bradycardia unresponsive to atropine, unstable heart block.

**Q52: What is the initial mA setting for transcutaneous pacing?**
A: Start at 50mA, increase until electrical capture (widened QRS) then mechanical capture (pulse).

**Q53: Why give analgesia/sedation for transcutaneous pacing?**
A: Procedure is extremely painful due to muscle contraction.

**Q54: What are indications for cardioversion?**
A: Unstable tachyarrhythmia (AF, flutter, SVT, VT with pulse).

**Q55: What energy for synchronized cardioversion of AF?**
A: 120-200J biphasic (start 120-150J).

**Q56: What energy for synchronized cardioversion of stable VT?**
A: 100J biphasic initially.

**Q57: What energy for defibrillation of VF/pulseless VT?**
A: 120-200J biphasic (max available), 360J monophasic.

**Q58: Why synchronize cardioversion?**
A: To avoid R-on-T phenomenon which can precipitate VF.

**Q59: When should you NOT synchronize?**
A: Pulseless VT/VF (defibrillate immediately), polymorphic VT (treat as VF).

**Q60: What pad placement for cardioversion?**
A: Anterolateral (standard) or anteroposterior (alternative).

## Section 7: Lumbar Puncture

**Q61: What are indications for lumbar puncture?**
A: Suspected meningitis/encephalitis, SAH with negative CT, idiopathic intracranial hypertension, Guillain-Barre.

**Q62: What are contraindications for LP?**
A: Increased ICP with mass effect, coagulopathy, infection at site, spinal cord compression.

**Q63: At what level do you perform LP?**
A: L3-L4 or L4-L5 (below conus medullaris which ends at L1-L2).

**Q64: How do you identify the correct interspace?**
A: Tuffier's line (intercristal line) connecting iliac crests = L4 spinous process.

**Q65: What position for LP?**
A: Lateral decubitus (fetal position) or sitting, flexed spine.

**Q66: What opening pressure is normal?**
A: 10-20 cm H2O (lateral decubitus).

**Q67: What opening pressure suggests IIH?**
A: >25 cm H2O.

**Q68: What CSF findings suggest bacterial meningitis?**
A: High protein, low glucose (<40% serum), neutrophilic pleocytosis, positive Gram stain.

**Q69: What CSF findings suggest viral meningitis?**
A: Normal/mild high protein, normal glucose, lymphocytic pleocytosis.

**Q70: What CSF findings suggest SAH?**
A: RBCs in all tubes, xanthochromia (after 12hrs), elevated opening pressure.

## Section 8: Arthrocentesis & Reductions

**Q71: What are indications for arthrocentesis?**
A: Suspected septic arthritis, crystal arthropathy, hemarthrosis, therapeutic drainage.

**Q72: What are contraindications for arthrocentesis?**
A: Overlying cellulitis, bacteremia, coagulopathy (relative).

**Q73: What synovial fluid WBC count suggests septic arthritis?**
A: >50,000 cells/uL with >75% neutrophils.

**Q74: How do you diagnose gout on arthrocentesis?**
A: Negatively birefringent needle-shaped crystals under polarized light.

**Q75: How do you diagnose pseudogout?**
A: Positively birefringent rhomboid crystals.

**Q76: What is the Milch maneuver?**
A: Shoulder dislocation reduction: abduct arm overhead, apply traction, external rotation.

**Q77: What is the Hennepin technique?**
A: Elbow reduction for posterior dislocation: supinate forearm, apply gentle traction while extending elbow.

**Q78: How do you reduce a nursemaid's elbow?**
A: Hyperpronation or supination-flexion technique with palpable click.

**Q79: What is the Ottawa ankle rule?**
A: X-ray if: bone tenderness at malleoli/base of 5th met/navicular OR inability to bear weight 4 steps.

**Q80: What neurovascular structure is at risk in knee dislocation?**
A: Popliteal artery (must check ABI, angiography if abnormal).

## Section 9: Ultrasound-Guided Procedures

**Q81: What are advantages of US-guided central lines?**
A: Higher success rate, fewer complications, fewer attempts, faster time.

**Q82: What probe frequency for vascular access?**
A: High-frequency linear probe (10-15 MHz).

**Q83: What is the difference between long-axis and short-axis approach?**
A: Long-axis: needle visualized entire length. Short-axis: needle seen as dot in cross-section.

**Q84: What US finding confirms IJ vein?**
A: Compressible, lateral to carotid, increases with Valsalva.

**Q85: What is FAST exam?**
A: Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma: pericardial, perihepatic, perisplenic, pelvic views.

**Q86: What does free fluid look like on FAST?**
A: Anechoic (black) areas in dependent spaces.

**Q87: What is lung sliding?**
A: Shimmering movement of visceral against parietal pleura (rules out pneumothorax).

**Q88: What is the barcode/sign?**
A: M-mode finding in pneumothorax: straight lines (absent lung sliding).

**Q89: What is the seashore/sign?**
A: M-mode normal finding: sandy beach pattern (lung sliding present).

**Q90: What is B-lines?**
A: Vertical hyperechoic artifacts from pleura to bottom of screen (pulmonary edema, ARDS).

## Section 10: Emergency Wound Management

**Q91: When should wounds not be primarily closed?**
A: >12hrs old (body) or >6hrs (face/hands), bite wounds (except face), contaminated wounds, crush injuries.

**Q92: What is the tensile strength timeline for wounds?**
A: 1 week: 10%, 2 weeks: 25%, 1 month: 50%, 3 months: 80% of original.

**Q93: When do you remove sutures from face?**
A: 3-5 days (excellent blood supply, cosmetic concern).

**Q94: When do you remove sutures from extremities?**
A: 10-14 days.

**Q95: When do you remove sutures from trunk?**
A: 7-10 days.

**Q96: What suture size for face?**
A: 6-0 or 7-0 non-absorbable.

**Q97: What suture size for extremities?**
A: 4-0 or 5-0.

**Q98: What suture size for scalp?**
A: 3-0 or 4-0, staples acceptable.

**Q99: What is the local anesthetic maximum dose for lidocaine?**
A: 4.5 mg/kg without epi, 7 mg/kg with epinephrine.

**Q100: What is the local anesthetic maximum dose for bupivacaine?**
A: 2 mg/kg (longer duration, more cardiotoxic).
