# Procedures & Airway Management: Comprehensive Viva Questions (200 Q&A)

## Section 1: Airway Management - Assessment & Basic Techniques (Questions 1-25)

### Subheading: Airway Assessment
**Q1: What are the predictors of a difficult airway?**
A: LEMON law: Look externally, Evaluate 3-3-2 rule, Mallampati score, Obstruction, Neck mobility.

**Q2: What is the 3-3-2 rule?**
A: 3 fingers mouth opening, 3 fingers hyoid-mental distance, 2 fingers thyroid-to-mouth floor distance.

**Q3: What does Mallampati Class III indicate?**
A: Only soft palate visible; predicts difficult laryngoscopy.

**Q4: What does Mallampati Class IV indicate?**
A: Only hard palate visible; highly predictive of difficult airway.

**Q5: What is the thyromental distance and its significance?**
A: Distance from thyroid notch to mentum; <6cm (3 fingerbreadths) suggests difficult airway.

**Q6: What neck pathology suggests difficult airway?**
A: Short/thick neck, limited neck extension, prior radiation/surgery, trauma.

**Q7: What is the significance of beard in airway management?**
A: Poor mask seal for bag-valve-mask ventilation.

**Q8: How do you assess for difficult BVM ventilation?**
A: MOANS: Mask seal, Obesity/Obstruction, Age (>55), No teeth, Stiff lungs.

**Q9: What is the significance of "No Teeth" in BVM?**
A: Difficulty achieving mask seal; may need to pack mouth or use special technique.

**Q10: What makes lung compliance "stiff"?**
A: Asthma, COPD, pulmonary edema, ARDS, pneumothorax.

**Q11: What is the significance of limited mouth opening?**
A: May prevent direct laryngoscopy; consider video/fiberoptic.

**Q12: What airway anomalies suggest difficulty?**
A: Micrognathia, macroglossia, trismus, facial trauma.

**Q13: What is the significance of prior difficult airway history?**
A: Strongest predictor of future difficult airway.

**Q14: How do you assess neck mobility?**
A: Ask patient to touch chin to chest; inability suggests difficult intubation.

**Q15: What is the significance of obesity in airway?**
A: Difficult BVM, rapid desaturation, difficult laryngoscopy.

**Q16: What is the ramp position?**
A: Elevating head/torso to align external auditory meatus with sternal notch.

**Q17: Who benefits most from ramp positioning?**
A: Obese patients, pregnant patients.

**Q18: What is sniffing position?**
A: Neck flexion + head extension; optimal for direct laryngoscopy in non-obese.

**Q19: What is the significance of stridor?**
A: Upper airway obstruction; may indicate difficult/failed intubation risk.

**Q20: What is the significance of hoarseness?**
A: Vocal cord pathology, mass, nerve palsy.

**Q21: What pre-oxygenation method is preferred?**
A: Tight-fitting mask with 100% O2 for 3-5 minutes or 8 vital capacity breaths.

**Q22: What is the goal of pre-oxygenation?**
A: Denitrogenate lungs, create oxygen reserve (EtO2 >90%).

**Q23: What is apneic oxygenation?**
A: Nasal cannula at 15L/min during laryngoscopy to prolong safe apnea time.

**Q24: What is the significance of SpO2 dropping during intubation?**
A: Hypoxia; stop attempt, re-oxygenate immediately.

**Q25: What is the "safe apnea time"?**
A: Time from apnea onset to desaturation; shorter in obese, pediatric, pregnant patients.

## Section 2: Rapid Sequence Intubation (RSI) (Questions 26-55)

### Subheading: RSI Medications - Induction Agents
**Q26: What are the 7 P's of RSI?**
A: Preparation, Pre-oxygenation, Pretreatment, Paralysis with Induction, Protection/Positioning, Placement, Post-intubation.

**Q27: What is the induction agent of choice for hemodynamically unstable patients?**
A: Etomidate (0.3 mg/kg).

**Q28: What is the main side effect of Etomidate?**
A: Adrenal suppression (single dose controversial significance).

**Q29: What is the induction agent of choice for asthmatics?**
A: Ketamine (1-2 mg/kg); bronchodilatory properties.

**Q30: What are the side effects of Ketamine?**
A: Increased HR/BP, increased secretions, emergence reactions, increased ICP (controversial).

**Q31: What is the induction agent for known difficult airway where spontaneous breathing must be maintained?**
A: Low-dose Ketamine or Dexmedetomidine; avoid paralytics initially.

**Q32: What is Propofol dosing for induction?**
A: 1-2 mg/kg; potent vasodilator.

**Q33: When is Propofol contraindicated?**
A: Hemodynamic instability, hypotension.

**Q34: What is Thiopental?**
A: Barbiturate; rarely used now due to hypotension/long half-life.

**Q35: What is Methohexital?**
A: Barbiturate for ECT/seizures; short acting.

**Q36: What induction agent is neuroprotective?**
A: Propofol, Thiopental (reduce cerebral metabolic demand).

**Q37: What is the role of Fentanyl in RSI?**
A: Analgesia, blunts sympathetic response to laryngoscopy.

**Q38: What is the dosing for Fentanyl in RSI?**
A: 1-3 mcg/kg (give 3 min before induction).

**Q39: What is Lidocaine used for in RSI?**
A: Blunts ICP rise, bronchospasm (evidence weak but commonly used).

**Q40: What is the dose of Lidocaine for RSI?**
A: 1.5 mg/kg IV 3 min before.

**Q41: When is pretreatment with Atropine indicated?**
A: Pediatrics <1 year (prevents bradycardia from succinylcholine).

**Q42: What is the dose of Atropine for pediatric RSI?**
A: 0.02 mg/kg (min 0.1mg, max 0.5mg).

**Q43: When is defasciculating dose used?**
A: Before Succinylcholine to prevent fasciculations (controversial).

**Q44: What is the defasciculating agent?**
A: Rocuronium 0.06 mg/kg or Vecuronium 0.01 mg/kg.

**Q45: What is the onset of Succinylcholine?**
A: 30-60 seconds.

**Q46: What is the duration of Succinylcholine?**
A: 5-10 minutes.

**Q47: What is the dose of Succinylcholine?**
A: 1-1.5 mg/kg IV (2-3 mg/kg IM if no access).

**Q48: What are absolute contraindications to Succinylcholine?**
A: Known hyperkalemia, burns >24h old, crush injury >24h, denervation injuries, malignant hyperthermia history.

**Q49: Why is Succinylcholine dangerous in burns/crush?**
A: Upregulation of extrajunctional ACh receptors → massive K+ release → cardiac arrest.

**Q50: What is Rocuronium dosing for RSI?**
A: 1-1.2 mg/kg (rapid sequence dose).

**Q51: What is the onset of high-dose Rocuronium?**
A: 45-60 seconds.

**Q52: What is the duration of Rocuronium?**
A: 60-90 minutes.

**Q53: What is the reversal agent for Rocuronium?**
A: Sugammadex (16 mg/kg for immediate reversal).

**Q54: What is the advantage of Rocuronium over Succinylcholine?**
A: No hyperkalemia risk, longer duration (good for prolonged procedures).

**Q55: What is the disadvantage of Rocuronium?**
A: Longer duration if intubation fails (cannot wake up quickly).

## Section 3: Intubation Techniques & Devices (Questions 56-85)

### Subheading: Direct & Video Laryngoscopy
**Q56: What is the Macintosh blade?**
A: Curved blade; placed in vallecula, lifts epiglottis indirectly.

**Q57: What is the Miller blade?**
A: Straight blade; placed under epiglottis, lifts directly.

**Q58: When is Miller blade preferred?**
A: Infants (large floppy epiglottis), anterior larynx.

**Q59: What is the BURP maneuver?**
A: Backward, Upward, Rightward Pressure on thyroid cartilage to improve view.

**Q60: What is the Cormack-Lehane grading system?**
A: Grade 1: Full cords; Grade 2: Partial cords; Grade 3: Epiglottis only; Grade 4: Nothing.

**Q61: What is a Video Laryngoscope (VL)?**
A: Blade with camera; improves glottic view, especially in difficult airways.

**Q62: What are types of VL?**
A: Hyperangulated (Glidescope, C-MAC D) vs Standard geometry (C-MAC 3).

**Q63: What is the advantage of VL?**
A: Better view, less cervical spine movement, teaching tool.

**Q64: What is the disadvantage of VL?**
A: Tube delivery can be difficult (need stylet), cost, fogging.

**Q65: What is the importance of stylet in VL?**
A: Essential for hyperangulated blades to navigate curve.

**Q66: What is the "hockey stick" shape?**
A: Stylet bent at distal end for VL intubation.

**Q67: What is Fiberoptic Intubation?**
A: Flexible scope through nose/mouth; gold standard for anticipated difficult airway.

**Q68: When is awake fiberoptic indicated?**
A: Known difficult airway, unstable C-spine, airway mass/obstruction.

**Q69: What is required for awake fiberoptic?**
A: Topical anesthesia (lidocaine), sedation (dexmedetomidine/ketamine), patient cooperation.

**Q70: What is the Lighted Stylet (Lightwand)?**
A: Transillumination device; useful when blood/vomit obscures view.

**Q71: What is Digital Intubation?**
A: Using fingers to guide tube; rescue technique.

**Q72: What is Blind Nasotracheal Intubation?**
A: Passing tube through nose listening for breath sounds; rarely done now.

**Q73: When is nasotracheal intubation contraindicated?**
A: Basilar skull fracture, coagulopathy, nasal obstruction.

**Q74: What size ETT for adult male?**
A: 7.5-8.0 mm ID.

**Q75: What size ETT for adult female?**
A: 7.0-7.5 mm ID.

**Q76: How do you confirm ETT depth?**
A: 21 cm at lips (female), 23 cm at lips (male).

**Q77: What is the formula for pediatric ETT size?**
A: (Age/4) + 4 for uncuffed; (Age/4) + 3.5 for cuffed.

**Q78: What is the formula for pediatric ETT depth?**
A: 3 x ETT size (at lips) or (Age/2) + 12.

**Q79: What is the cuff pressure goal?**
A: 20-30 cm H2O.

**Q80: What happens if cuff pressure is too high?**
A: Tracheal ischemia, stenosis, nerve damage.

**Q81: What happens if cuff pressure is too low?**
A: Aspiration, air leak, inadequate ventilation.

**Q82: What is the bougie (gum elastic bougie)?**
A: Introducer to facilitate difficult intubation.

**Q83: How do you use a bougie?**
A: Insert through cords, feel "clicks" (tracheal rings), advance, rail ETT over it.

**Q84: What is the "hold-up sign"?**
A: Bougie stops at carina (confirms tracheal placement).

**Q85: What is Exchange Catheter?**
A: Used to replace ETT in difficult airway without losing access.

## Section 4: Confirmation & Post-Intubation Management (Questions 86-110)

**Q86: What is the gold standard for confirming ETT placement?**
A: Continuous waveform capnography (EtCO2).

**Q87: What if EtCO2 is not detected?**
A: Assume esophageal intubation; remove tube and re-oxygenate.

**Q88: Can EtCO2 be false negative?**
A: Yes: Cardiac arrest (low flow), massive PE, severe asthma (air trapping).

**Q89: What is secondary confirmation methods?**
A: Chest rise, bilateral breath sounds, fogging in tube, CXR.

**Q90: What does CXR confirm post-intubation?**
A: Tube depth (above carina), rule out pneumothorax.

**Q91: Where should ETT tip be relative to carina?**
A: 3-5 cm above carina.

**Q92: What is the initial ventilator setting for newly intubated?**
A: AC/VC, Rate 12-14, TV 6-8 mL/kg IBW, FiO2 100% then titrate.

**Q93: What is Lung Protective Ventilation?**
A: Low tidal volume (6 mL/kg IBW), plateau pressure <30.

**Q94: What is PEEP and why use it?**
A: Positive End-Expiratory Pressure; prevents alveolar collapse, improves oxygenation.

**Q95: What is typical initial PEEP?**
A: 5 cm H2O.

**Q96: What is Auto-PEEP?**
A: Air trapping from incomplete exhalation; common in asthma/COPD.

**Q97: How do you manage Auto-PEEP?**
A: Decrease rate, increase expiratory time, decrease TV.

**Q98: What sedation agents are used post-intubation?**
A: Propofol, Fentanyl, Midazolam, Dexmedetomidine, Ketamine.

**Q99: What is the goal of post-intubation sedation?**
A: Comfort, ventilator synchrony, prevent self-extubation.

**Q100: What paralysis may be needed post-intubation?**
A: Severe ARDS (proning), elevated ICP, status asthmaticus.

**Q101: What is Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP) prevention?**
A: Head of bed >30°, oral care, sedation vacation, DVT prophylaxis.

**Q102: What is accidental extubation management?**
A: Assess patient, re-oxygenate, reintubate if needed.

**Q103: What is tube occlusion signs?**
A: High pressure alarm, inability to pass suction catheter, respiratory distress.

**Q104: What is cuff leak test?**
A: Deflate cuff, check for air leak around tube; predicts post-extubation stridor.

**Q105: When is extubation appropriate?**
A: Resolved underlying issue, adequate oxygenation, strong cough, passing leak test.

**Q106: What is post-extubation stridor treatment?**
A: Racemic epinephrine, steroids, humidified O2, reintubation if severe.

**Q107: What is tracheostomy indication?**
A: Prolonged ventilation (>1-2 weeks), upper airway obstruction, secretion management.

**Q108: What is percutaneous tracheostomy?**
A: Bedside procedure using Seldinger technique.

**Q109: What is surgical tracheostomy?**
A: OR procedure; preferred in emergencies/anatomic difficulties.

**Q110: What are complications of tracheostomy?**
A: Bleeding, infection, tube displacement, tracheal stenosis, fistula.

## Section 5: Difficult Airway Algorithms & Rescue (Questions 111-140)

### Subheading: ASA Difficult Airway Algorithm
**Q111: What is the first decision in difficult airway algorithm?**
A: Anticipated difficult vs Unanticipated difficult.

**Q112: What is the approach for anticipated difficult airway?**
A: Awake intubation (fiberoptic) or maintain spontaneous ventilation.

**Q113: What is the approach for unanticipated difficult airway?**
A: Call for help, optimize position, try alternative devices (VL, bougie).

**Q114: What is "Can't Intubate, Can't Oxygenate" (CICO)?**
A: Failed intubation + failed BVM; emergency front-of-neck access needed.

**Q115: What are rescue ventilation techniques?**
A: Two-person BVM, oral/nasal airway, LMA/King supraglottic.

**Q116: What is LMA (Laryngeal Mask Airway)?**
A: Supraglottic device; bridge to intubation or rescue.

**Q117: Can you intubate through LMA?**
A: Yes, using fiberoptic or blind technique (Fastrach LMA).

**Q118: What is King LT?**
A: Esophageal-tracheal double lumen supraglottic airway.

**Q119: When is supraglottic airway contraindicated?**
A: Full stomach (relative), known esophageal disease, pharyngeal pathology.

**Q120: What is the next step if supraglottic fails?**
A: Emergency front-of-neck access (eFONA).

**Q121: What is Needle Cricothyrotomy?**
A: Large bore (12-14G) needle through cricothyroid membrane; temporizing.

**Q122: How long is needle cric valid?**
A: 30-45 minutes (provides oxygenation, limited ventilation).

**Q123: What equipment is needed for needle cric?**
A: Large bore angiocath, syringe, high-pressure O2 source (Jet ventilator).

**Q124: What is Surgical Cricothyrotomy?**
A: Incision through cricothyroid membrane, insert cuffed tube.

**Q125: What size tube for surgical cric?**
A: 5-6 mm cuffed ETT or Shiley trach.

**Q126: What are landmarks for cricothyrotomy?**
A: Thyroid cartilage, cricoid cartilage, cricothyroid membrane between them.

**Q127: What is the "No-Neck" technique?**
A: For obese patients; vertical incision, blunt dissection to find membrane.

**Q128: What age is surgical cric contraindicated?**
A: <10-12 years (use needle cric instead).

**Q129: Why avoid surgical cric in young children?**
A: Risk of subglottic stenosis, small anatomy.

**Q130: What is retrograde intubation?**
A: Wire passed through cricothyroid membrane, retrieved from mouth, rail ETT.

**Q131: What is transtracheal jet ventilation (TTJV)?**
A: High-pressure O2 through needle; risk of barotrauma.

**Q132: What is the complication of TTJV?**
A: Pneumothorax, subcutaneous emphysema.

**Q133: When do you convert cricothyrotomy to tracheostomy?**
A: Within 24-72 hours (reduce subglottic stenosis risk).

**Q134: What is Vortex Approach?**
A: Cognitive aid for difficult airway: Green zone (oxygenation) vs Red zone (CICO).

**Q135: What are the 3 non-invasive techniques in Vortex?**
A: BVM, Supraglottic, ETT.

**Q136: What if all 3 fail in Vortex?**
A: Declare CICO, proceed to eFONA.

**Q137: What is the role of cognitive aids in difficult airway?**
A: Reduce errors, guide decision-making under stress.

**Q138: What is Plan A, B, C, D in difficult airway?**
A: A: Primary plan (DL/VL); B: Secondary (SGA); C: Rescue (eFONA); D: Declaration of failure.

**Q139: When should you wake patient up instead of proceeding?**
A: If oxygenation maintained but intubation failing, patient stable.

**Q140: What factors decide "wake up" vs "press on"?**
A: Oxygenation status, urgency of surgery, patient stability.

## Section 6: Central Venous Access (Questions 141-165)

### Subheading: CVC Placement & Complications
**Q141: What are indications for central venous catheter?**
A: Vasopressors, poor peripheral access, TPN, hemodialysis, frequent blood draws.

**Q142: What are common CVC sites?**
A: Internal Jugular (IJ), Subclavian, Femoral.

**Q143: What is the preferred site for non-emergent CVC?**
A: Subclavian (lower infection/thrombosis risk).

**Q144: What is the preferred site for emergent CVC?**
A: Femoral (fast, easy) or IJ (ultrasound guidance).

**Q145: What is the advantage of ultrasound guidance?**
A: Higher success, fewer complications, fewer attempts.

**Q146: What structures to identify in US-guided IJ?**
A: IJ vein (compressible, lateral), Carotid artery (pulsatile, medial).

**Q147: What is the landmark for IJ approach?**
A: Apex of triangle formed by SCM heads and clavicle.

**Q148: What is the landmark for Subclavian approach?**
A: Infraclavicular, 1-2 cm below clavicle at mid-clavicular line.

**Q149: What is the major risk of Subclavian line?**
A: Pneumothorax.

**Q150: What is the major risk of IJ line?**
A: Carotid puncture.

**Q151: What is the major risk of Femoral line?**
A: Infection, DVT, retroperitoneal bleed.

**Q152: What is Seldinger technique?**
A: Needle → wire → dilator → catheter.

**Q153: What is the modified Seldinger?**
A: Through-needle catheter (less common now).

**Q154: How do you confirm CVC placement?**
A: Aspiration of blood, transduction of waveform, CXR.

**Q155: Where should CVC tip be located?**
A: SVC-RA junction (carina level on CXR).

**Q156: What is malposition of CVC?**
A: Into contralateral vein, azygos, internal mammary, ventricle.

**Q157: What is the complication of arterial puncture?**
A: Hematoma, pseudoaneurysm, stroke (if carotid).

**Q158: How do you manage accidental arterial cannulation?**
A: Remove if small needle; leave in if large dilator/catheter (call surgery).

**Q159: What is air embolism?**
A: Air enters vein → RV outflow obstruction.

**Q160: How do you prevent air embolism?**
A: Trendelenburg, Valsalva, occlusive dressing during insertion/removal.

**Q161: What is the treatment for air embolism?**
A: Left lateral decubitus + Trendelenburg (Durant maneuver), 100% O2, aspirate from CVC.

**Q162: What is catheter-related bloodstream infection (CRBSI)?**
A: Fever, positive cultures from CVC.

**Q163: How do you prevent CRBSI?**
A: Maximal barrier precautions, chlorhexidine skin prep, daily necessity review.

**Q164: What is thrombosis of CVC?**
A: Clot formation; swelling, pain, dysfunction.

**Q165: What is the management of CVC thrombosis?**
A: Anticoagulation, remove catheter if infected/non-functional.

## Section 7: Chest Procedures (Questions 166-185)

### Subheading: Thoracostomy & Pericardiocentesis
**Q166: What is the indication for Needle Decompression?**
A: Tension pneumothorax with hemodynamic instability.

**Q167: What is the landmark for needle decompression?**
A: 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line OR 4th/5th ICS, anterior axillary line.

**Q168: What length catheter is needed for needle decompression?**
A: ≥8 cm (standard 5cm often too short).

**Q169: What is the definitive treatment for pneumothorax?**
A: Tube thoracostomy (chest tube).

**Q170: What is the landmark for chest tube insertion?**
A: 4th/5th ICS, anterior to midaxillary line ("safe triangle").

**Q171: What size chest tube for pneumothorax?**
A: 24-28 Fr.

**Q172: What size chest tube for hemothorax?**
A: 32-40 Fr.

**Q173: What direction to aim chest tube?**
A: Apical for air, basal/posterior for fluid.

**Q174: What is the "finger sweep" in chest tube?**
A: Confirm intrapleural placement, break adhesions.

**Q175: What is water seal?**
A: One-way valve allowing air/fluid out, not in.

**Q176: What is suction setting for chest tube?**
A: -20 cm H2O.

**Q177: When do you remove chest tube?**
A: Lung re-expanded, no air leak, minimal drainage (<150-200mL/day).

**Q178: What is clamping chest tube?**
A: Generally contraindicated (risk tension PTX); only for brief troubleshooting.

**Q179: What are complications of chest tube?**
A: Malposition, bleeding, infection, re-expansion pulmonary edema.

**Q180: What is Re-expansion Pulmonary Edema?**
A: Non-cardiogenic edema after rapid lung re-expansion.

**Q181: What is Pericardiocentesis indication?**
A: Cardiac tamponade with hemodynamic compromise.

**Q182: What is the approach for pericardiocentesis?**
A: Subxiphoid (most common), apical, parasternal.

**Q183: What is the landmark for subxiphoid approach?**
A: Needle angled 45° toward left shoulder, below xiphoid.

**Q184: What guidance is preferred for pericardiocentesis?**
A: Ultrasound (safer, higher success).

**Q185: What is the sign of entering pericardial space?**
A: Flash of blood, improved hemodynamics, ST elevation resolution.

## Section 8: Cardiac & Other Procedures (Questions 186-200)

**Q186: What is Transcutaneous Pacing indication?**
A: Symptomatic bradycardia refractory to atropine.

**Q187: What is the energy for transcutaneous pacing?**
A: Start 50mA, increase until capture (visible twitch + QRS).

**Q188: What is sedation needed for transcutaneous pacing?**
A: Yes; painful procedure.

**Q189: What is Temporary Transvenous Pacing?**
A: Wire through vein to RV; more reliable than transcutaneous.

**Q190: What is Cardioversion indication?**
A: Unstable tachyarrhythmia (AFib, Flutter, SVT, VT with pulse).

**Q191: What is the energy for synchronized cardioversion?**
A: AFib: 120-200J biphasic; VT: 100J; start lower and escalate.

**Q192: Why synchronize in cardioversion?**
A: Avoid R-on-T phenomenon (precipitate VF).

**Q193: What is Defibrillation indication?**
A: VF, pulseless VT.

**Q194: What is the energy for defibrillation?**
A: Unsynchronized, max dose (200-360J biphasic).

**Q195: What is Lumbar Puncture indication?**
A: Suspected meningitis, SAH (if CT negative), idiopathic intracranial hypertension.

**Q196: What is the landmark for LP?**
A: L3-L4 or L4-L5 interspace (Tuffier's line = iliac crests).

**Q197: What is contraindication to LP?**
A: Increased ICP with mass effect, coagulopathy, skin infection.

**Q198: What is Arthrocentesis indication?**
A: Septic arthritis, crystal arthropathy, effusion relief.

**Q199: What is the key finding in septic arthritis?**
A: WBC >50,000 in synovial fluid, >90% neutrophils.

**Q200: What is the universal precaution for all procedures?**
A: Sterile technique, PPE, timeout, informed consent (if possible).
