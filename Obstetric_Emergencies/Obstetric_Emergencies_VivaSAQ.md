# Master Case: Obstetric Emergencies

## Section 1: First Trimester Emergencies

**Q1: What is the differential for first trimester bleeding?**
A: Ectopic pregnancy, threatened/inevitable/incomplete/complete abortion, molar pregnancy, subchorionic hemorrhage.

**Q2: What is the discriminatory zone for beta-hCG?**
A: 1500-2000 mIU/mL - above this level, intrauterine pregnancy should be visible on transvaginal ultrasound.

**Q3: What is the classic triad of ectopic pregnancy?**
A: Abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, adnexal mass (only present in ~50% of cases).

**Q4: What are risk factors for ectopic pregnancy?**
A: Prior ectopic, PID, tubal surgery, IUD, smoking, assisted reproduction, prior C-section.

**Q5: How do you diagnose ectopic pregnancy?**
A: Transvaginal ultrasound (no IUP with beta-hCG > discriminatory zone, adnexal mass), serial beta-hCG.

**Q6: What is the management of stable ectopic pregnancy?**
A: Methotrexate if criteria met (stable, <3.5cm, no fetal cardiac activity, beta-hCG <5000, reliable follow-up).

**Q7: What are contraindications to methotrexate?**
A: Hemodynamic instability, rupture, fetal cardiac activity, beta-hCG >5000, mass >3.5cm, breastfeeding, immunodeficiency, liver/renal disease.

**Q8: What is the methotrexate dosing protocol?**
A: 50 mg/m² IM single dose, check beta-hCG day 4 and 7, repeat if <15% decline.

**Q9: When do you operate on ectopic pregnancy?**
A: Hemodynamic instability, rupture, contraindications to methotrexate, failed medical management.

**Q10: What is a heterotopic pregnancy?**
A: Simultaneous intrauterine and ectopic pregnancy (rare, higher with ART).

## Section 2: Second & Third Trimester Bleeding

**Q11: What are causes of second/third trimester bleeding?**
A: Placenta previa, placental abruption, vasa previa, uterine rupture, cervical causes.

**Q12: What is placenta previa presentation?**
A: Painless bright red vaginal bleeding, often after intercourse or exam.

**Q13: How do you diagnose placenta previa?**
A: Transvaginal ultrasound (DO NOT perform digital exam until previa ruled out).

**Q14: How do you manage placenta previa?**
A: Stabilize, type & cross, avoid digital exams, C-section if complete previa or heavy bleeding.

**Q15: What is placental abruption presentation?**
A: Painful vaginal bleeding, uterine tenderness, contractions, fetal distress.

**Q16: What are risk factors for abruption?**
A: Hypertension, trauma, cocaine, smoking, prior abruption, premature rupture of membranes.

**Q17: Can ultrasound rule out abruption?**
A: NO - ultrasound has low sensitivity (up to 50% of abruptions not seen on US).

**Q18: How do you manage abruption?**
A: Stabilize mother, continuous fetal monitoring, deliver if unstable or fetal distress.

**Q19: What is vasa previa?**
A: Fetal vessels crossing cervical os (painless bleeding with fetal bradycardia after membrane rupture).

**Q20: What is the classic sign of vasa previa?**
A: APGAR staining test positive (fetal blood in vagina), fetal bradycardia after ROM.

## Section 3: Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy

**Q21: What defines gestational hypertension?**
A: BP ≥140/90 after 20 weeks without proteinuria or severe features.

**Q22: What defines preeclampsia?**
A: BP ≥140/90 after 20 weeks + proteinuria OR end-organ damage (thrombocytopenia, elevated LFTs, renal insufficiency, pulmonary edema, cerebral/visual symptoms).

**Q23: What defines severe preeclampsia?**
A: BP ≥160/110, thrombocytopenia <100K, elevated creatinine, elevated LFTs 2x normal, pulmonary edema, new headache/vision changes.

**Q24: What is HELLP syndrome?**
A: Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets - variant of severe preeclampsia.

**Q25: How do you treat severe preeclampsia?**
A: Magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis, antihypertensives (labetalol, hydralazine), delivery is definitive treatment.

**Q26: What is the magnesium sulfate regimen?**
A: 4-6g IV load over 20-30 min, then 1-2g/hr infusion.

**Q27: What are signs of magnesium toxicity?**
A: Loss of reflexes (first), respiratory depression (<12/min), cardiac arrest.

**Q28: How do you reverse magnesium toxicity?**
A: Stop infusion, calcium gluconate 1g IV.

**Q29: What antihypertensives are safe in pregnancy?**
A: Labetalol, hydralazine, nifedipine. Avoid ACE inhibitors, ARBs.

**Q30: What is eclampsia?**
A: New-onset seizures in preeclamptic patient.

## Section 4: Labor & Delivery Emergencies

**Q31: What is the definition of active labor?**
A: Regular contractions with cervical change, typically ≥6cm dilation.

**Q32: What is prolonged first stage of labor?**
A: <1.2 cm/hr dilation (nulliparous) or <1.5 cm/hr (multiparous) in active phase.

**Q33: What are the 3 P's of labor dystocia?**
A: Power (contractions), Passenger (fetal size/position), Pelvis (maternal anatomy).

**Q34: What is shoulder dystocia?**
A: After head delivers, anterior shoulder impacted behind pubic symphysis.

**Q35: What is the mnemonic for shoulder dystocia management?**
A: HELPERR: Help, Episiotomy, Legs (McRoberts), Pressure (suprapubic), Enter maneuvers, Remove posterior arm, Roll (Gaskin).

**Q36: What is the McRoberts maneuver?**
A: Hyperflex maternal thighs to abdomen, flattens sacrum, rotates symphysis.

**Q37: What is cord prolapse?**
A: Umbilical cord descends through cervix ahead of fetus (emergency).

**Q38: How do you manage cord prolapse?**
A: Call for help, elevate presenting part (hand in vagina), knee-chest position, emergent C-section.

**Q39: What is uterine rupture risk factor?**
A: Prior C-section (classical highest risk), induction/augmentation, multiparity, trauma.

**Q40: What are signs of uterine rupture?**
A: Sudden abdominal pain, loss of fetal station, fetal bradycardia, vaginal bleeding, hemodynamic collapse.

## Section 5: Postpartum Emergencies

**Q41: What is postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) definition?**
A: Blood loss >1000mL or bleeding with hemodynamic instability within 24hrs (early) or 24hrs-12 weeks (late).

**Q42: What are the 4 T's of PPH?**
A: Tone (uterine atony), Trauma (lacerations), Tissue (retained products), Thrombin (coagulopathy).

**Q43: What is most common cause of PPH?**
A: Uterine atony (70-80%).

**Q44: What is first-line management of uterine atony?**
A: Bimanual uterine massage, oxytocin infusion.

**Q45: What medications for refractory uterine atony?**
A: Methylergonovine (avoid in HTN), carboprost (avoid in asthma), misoprostol, tranexamic acid.

**Q46: What is the dose of methylergonovine?**
A: 0.2mg IM q2-4h (contraindicated in hypertension).

**Q47: What is the dose of carboprost (Hemabate)?**
A: 250 mcg IM q15-90min (max 2mg), contraindicated in asthma.

**Q48: What is tranexamic acid dose for PPH?**
A: 1g IV over 10 min (within 3hrs of birth).

**Q49: What is uterine inversion?**
A: Fundus collapses into endometrial cavity (emergency).

**Q50: How do you manage uterine inversion?**
A: Immediate manual replacement (Johnson maneuver), stop uterotonics, consider tocolytics, surgery if failed.

## Section 6: Fetal Monitoring & Distress

**Q51: What is normal fetal heart rate?**
A: 110-160 bpm.

**Q52: What is fetal bradycardia definition?**
A: FHR <110 bpm for ≥10 minutes.

**Q53: What is fetal tachycardia definition?**
A: FHR >160 bpm for ≥10 minutes.

**Q54: What are early decelerations?**
A: Mirror image of contractions (head compression, benign).

**Q55: What are late decelerations?**
A: Nadir after contraction peak (placental insufficiency, concerning).

**Q56: What are variable decelerations?**
A: Abrupt drops (cord compression), may benefit from amnioinfusion.

**Q57: What is Category III tracing?**
A: Absent variability + recurrent late/variable decels or bradycardia (requires immediate intervention).

**Q58: What is intrauterine resuscitation?**
A: Left lateral position, IV fluids, oxygen, stop oxytocin, treat hypotension.

**Q59: When do you proceed to emergent delivery for fetal distress?**
A: Persistent Category III tracing, prolonged bradycardia, scalp pH <7.20.

**Q60: What is meconium aspiration syndrome?**
A: Respiratory distress from meconium-stained amniotic fluid aspiration.

## Section 7: Medical Emergencies in Pregnancy

**Q61: How does pregnancy affect airway management?**
A: Increased edema, decreased FRC, increased O2 consumption, rapid desaturation, difficult intubation.

**Q62: What is the preferred vasopressor in pregnancy?**
A: Phenylephrine or norepinephrine (maintain uteroplacental perfusion).

**Q63: What is AFE (amniotic fluid embolism)?**
A: Sudden cardiovascular collapse, coagulopathy, hypoxia during/after labor.

**Q64: How do you treat AFE?**
A: Supportive care (ABCs), massive transfusion, deliver fetus if undelivered.

**Q65: What is perimortem C-section indication?**
A: Pregnant patient (>20 weeks) in cardiac arrest, no ROSC after 4 min.

**Q66: What is the goal time for perimortem C-section?**
A: Deliver within 5 minutes of arrest (improves maternal AND fetal outcomes).

**Q67: Where do you make incision for perimortem C-section?**
A: Vertical midline (fastest), vertical uterine incision.

**Q68: What antibiotics for GBS prophylaxis?**
A: Penicillin G (alternatives: ampicillin, cefazolin, clindamycin, vancomycin).

**Q69: What is chorioamnionitis presentation?**
A: Maternal fever, uterine tenderness, foul amniotic fluid, fetal/maternal tachycardia.

**Q70: How do you treat chorioamnionitis?**
A: Broad-spectrum antibiotics (ampicillin + gentamicin), deliver fetus.

## Section 8: Trauma in Pregnancy

**Q71: What is the leading cause of fetal death in trauma?**
A: Maternal death (prevent by aggressive maternal resuscitation).

**Q72: How do you position pregnant trauma patient?**
A: Left lateral tilt (displace uterus off IVC) after 20 weeks.

**Q73: What imaging is safe in pregnancy?**
A: X-rays with shielding, CT if necessary (fetal radiation risk low with proper shielding), MRI.

**Q74: What is Kleihauer-Betke test?**
A: Detects fetomaternal hemorrhage (guides Rhogam dosing).

**Q75: When do you give Rhogam?**
A: Rh-negative mother with trauma, bleeding, procedures (dose based on KB test).

**Q76: What is placental abruption risk after trauma?**
A: Can occur up to 24-48hrs post-injury (monitor 4+ hours).

**Q77: What is the threshold for fetal monitoring after trauma?**
A: All viable pregnancies (>20-24 weeks) need 4+ hours monitoring.

**Q78: What are indications for C-section after trauma?**
A: Maternal instability, placental abruption, uterine rupture, fetal distress.

**Q79: Can you perform FAST in pregnancy?**
A: Yes, but less sensitive (gravid uterus limits views).

**Q80: What is the mortality rate for pregnant trauma patients?**
A: Similar to non-pregnant with appropriate care (aggressive maternal resuscitation is key).

## Section 9: Toxicology in Pregnancy

**Q81: Is acetaminophen safe in pregnancy?**
A: Yes, first-line analgesic/antipyretic.

**Q82: Are NSAIDs safe in pregnancy?**
A: Avoid in third trimester (premature ductus arteriosus closure, oligohydramnios).

**Q83: What antibiotics are contraindicated?**
A: Tetracyclines (teeth discoloration), fluoroquinolones (cartilage), aminoglycosides (ototoxicity - relative).

**Q84: What antiemetics are safe?**
A: Doxylamine-pyridoxine (first-line), ondansetron, metoclopramide.

**Q85: How do you treat hyperemesis gravidarum?**
A: IV fluids, thiamine (before dextrose), antiemetics, nutritional support.

**Q86: What is the concern with warfarin in pregnancy?**
A: Teratogenic (warfarin embryopathy), use LMWH instead.

**Q87: Are opioids safe in pregnancy?**
A: Use cautiously (neonatal withdrawal), but treat pain appropriately.

**Q88: What is the management of opioid overdose in pregnancy?**
A: Naloxone (may precipitate withdrawal), supportive care, fetal monitoring.

**Q89: Can you use activated charcoal in pregnancy?**
A: Yes, if indicated (doesn't cross placenta).

**Q90: What is the concern with cocaine in pregnancy?**
A: Placental abruption, preterm labor, fetal growth restriction.

## Section 10: Special Topics

**Q91: What is mirror syndrome?**
A: Maternal edema/hypertension associated with fetal hydrops (resolves with delivery).

**Q92: What is supine hypotensive syndrome?**
A: Uterine compression of IVC causing hypotension (relieved by left lateral tilt).

**Q93: What is tocolysis used for?**
A: Temporarily delay delivery (steroids, transfer) - not for long-term prevention.

**Q94: What tocolytics are available?**
A: Nifedipine, indomethacin (<32 weeks), terbutaline, magnesium sulfate.

**Q95: When is tocolysis contraindicated?**
A: Chorioamnionitis, severe preeclampsia, fetal distress, significant bleeding, advanced labor.

**Q96: What is the role of steroids in preterm labor?**
A: Betamethasone 12mg IM x2 doses 24hrs apart (24-34 weeks) for fetal lung maturity.

**Q97: When do you give neuroprotection?**
A: Magnesium sulfate <32 weeks for cerebral palsy reduction.

**Q98: What is cervical cerclage?**
A: Surgical closure of cervix for cervical insufficiency.

**Q99: What is PPROM?**
A: Preterm premature rupture of membranes (<37 weeks).

**Q100: How do you manage PPROM?**
A: Antibiotics, steroids, expectant management vs delivery based on gestational age and complications.
