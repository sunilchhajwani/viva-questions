# Obstetric Emergency Medicine: Comprehensive Viva Questions (200 Q&A)

## Section 1: First Trimester Emergencies (Questions 1-25)

### Subheading: Ectopic Pregnancy & Early Pregnancy Bleeding
**Q1: What is the classic triad of ectopic pregnancy?**
A: Abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, and adnexal mass (though only present in ~50% of cases).

**Q2: What is the most common location for ectopic pregnancy?**
A: Ampulla of the fallopian tube (>70%).

**Q3: What are the risk factors for ectopic pregnancy?**
A: Prior ectopic, PID, tubal surgery, IUD use, assisted reproductive technology, smoking.

**Q4: What is the discriminatory zone for beta-hCG in transvaginal ultrasound?**
A: 1500-2000 mIU/mL; above this level, an intrauterine pregnancy should be visible.

**Q5: What is the management of a stable unruptured ectopic pregnancy?**
A: Methotrexate (single or multi-dose protocol) if criteria met.

**Q6: What are the contraindications to Methotrexate for ectopic pregnancy?**
A: Hemodynamic instability, rupture, fetal cardiac activity, mass >3.5-4cm, hCG >5000, breastfeeding, immunodeficiency, liver/renal disease.

**Q7: What is the follow-up protocol after Methotrexate administration?**
A: Check hCG on Day 4 and Day 7; if <15% drop between days 4-7, give second dose.

**Q8: What symptoms suggest ruptured ectopic pregnancy?**
A: Sudden severe pain, syncope, shoulder tip pain (hemoperitoneum), hemodynamic instability.

**Q9: What is the immediate management of ruptured ectopic pregnancy?**
A: ABCs, IV fluids, blood transfusion, emergent surgical consultation (laparoscopy/laparotomy).

**Q10: What is a heterotopic pregnancy?**
A: Simultaneous intrauterine and extrauterine pregnancy; rare in spontaneous conception, higher with IVF.

**Q11: How is heterotopic pregnancy diagnosed?**
A: Visualization of IUP on US does NOT rule out ectopic; look for adnexal mass/free fluid.

**Q12: What is the management of heterotopic pregnancy?**
A: Systemic MTX contraindicated (kills IUP); requires local injection or surgical removal of ectopic.

**Q13: What is the differential diagnosis for first trimester bleeding?**
A: Ectopic, miscarriage (threatened, inevitable, incomplete, complete, missed), molar pregnancy, subchorionic hemorrhage.

**Q14: What defines a threatened abortion?**
A: Vaginal bleeding before 20 weeks with closed cervical os and viable IUP.

**Q15: What is the management of threatened abortion?**
A: Reassurance, pelvic rest, follow-up; Rhogam if Rh-negative.

**Q16: What defines an inevitable abortion?**
A: Bleeding with open cervical os; pregnancy loss is unavoidable.

**Q17: What is an incomplete abortion?**
A: Passage of some products of conception (POC) with retained tissue; open os.

**Q18: What is a missed abortion?**
A: Fetal demise without expulsion; closed os, no cardiac activity on US.

**Q19: What is the management of incomplete/missed abortion?**
A: Expectant, medical (Misoprostol), or surgical (D&C) management.

**Q20: What is Rh isoimmunization and how is it prevented?**
A: Maternal antibodies attack fetal RBCs; prevented with Rho(D) immune globulin (Rhogam) at 28wks and after bleeding events.

**Q21: When should Rhogam be given in first trimester bleeding?**
A: All Rh-negative women with bleeding, ectopic, abortion, or instrumentation (dose depends on gestational age).

**Q22: What is a molar pregnancy (Gestational Trophoblastic Disease)?**
A: Abnormal proliferation of trophoblasts; Complete (no fetal tissue, 46XX) vs Partial (fetal tissue, triploid).

**Q23: What are the clinical features of molar pregnancy?**
A: "Snowstorm" on US, hCG markedly elevated (>100k), uterine size >dates, hyperemesis, preeclampsia <20wks.

**Q24: What is the treatment for molar pregnancy?**
A: Suction D&C, serial hCG monitoring for choriocarcinoma.

**Q25: What is the concern with ovarian cysts in early pregnancy?**
A: Corpus luteum cyst supports pregnancy until 10-12 weeks; torsion risk; avoid removal unless necessary.

## Section 2: Second & Third Trimester Bleeding (Questions 26-50)

### Subheading: Placenta Previa & Abruption
**Q26: What is Placenta Previa?**
A: Placenta implants over or near internal cervical os.

**Q27: What is the classic presentation of placenta previa?**
A: Painless bright red vaginal bleeding in third trimester.

**Q28: What is the absolute contraindication in suspected placenta previa?**
A: Digital cervical examination (can provoke massive hemorrhage).

**Q29: How is placenta previa diagnosed?**
A: Transvaginal or transabdominal ultrasound (safe).

**Q30: What is the management of stable placenta previa?**
A: Pelvic rest, close monitoring, steroids for lung maturity if preterm, C-section delivery.

**Q31: What is Vasa Previa?**
A: Fetal vessels run over cervical os unprotected by placenta or cord; rupture causes fetal exsanguination.

**Q32: What is the classic triad of vasa previa rupture?**
A: Membrane rupture, painless bleeding, fetal bradycardia/sinusoidal pattern.

**Q33: What is the management of vasa previa?**
A: Emergent C-section; high fetal mortality if undiagnosed.

**Q34: What is Placental Abruption?**
A: Premature separation of normally implanted placenta from uterine wall.

**Q35: What are the risk factors for abruption?**
A: Hypertension/preeclampsia, cocaine use, trauma, smoking, prior abruption, PROM.

**Q36: What is the classic presentation of abruption?**
A: Painful vaginal bleeding, uterine tenderness, hypertonus/tetany, fetal distress.

**Q37: Can abruption occur without visible bleeding?**
A: Yes (concealed abruption); blood trapped behind placenta.

**Q38: How is abruption diagnosed?**
A: Clinical diagnosis; US has low sensitivity (only sees large retroplacental clots).

**Q39: What is the management of abruption?**
A: Stabilize mother (2 large bore IVs, blood), continuous fetal monitoring, deliver if unstable/non-reassuring.

**Q40: What is Couvelaire Uterus?**
A: Blood infiltrates myometrium causing purple discoloration; may cause uterine atony.

**Q41: What coagulopathy is associated with severe abruption?**
A: Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC).

**Q42: What is Uterine Rupture?**
A: Full-thickness disruption of uterine wall; catastrophic obstetric emergency.

**Q43: What is the biggest risk factor for uterine rupture?**
A: Prior C-section (trial of labor after cesarean - TOLAC).

**Q44: What are the signs of uterine rupture?**
A: Sudden severe pain, loss of fetal station, cessation of contractions, fetal bradycardia, hemodynamic collapse.

**Q45: What is the management of uterine rupture?**
A: Emergent laparotomy, delivery, repair or hysterectomy, massive transfusion.

**Q46: What is Marginal Sinus Rupture?**
A: Bleeding from edge of placenta; less severe than abruption/previa.

**Q47: What is the differential for third trimester bleeding?**
A: Previa, Abruption, Vasa Previa, Uterine Rupture, Labor ("bloody show"), Cervical pathology.

**Q48: What initial labs are needed for OB hemorrhage?**
A: CBC, Type & Cross (4-6 units), Coags (PT/PTT/Fibrinogen), Kleihauer-Betke (if trauma/Rh-).

**Q49: What is the role of Kleihauer-Betke test?**
A: Quantifies fetomaternal hemorrhage to calculate additional Rhogam dose in Rh-negative mothers.

**Q50: What is the definition of Massive Obstetric Hemorrhage?**
A: >1000mL blood loss or signs of hypovolemia; requires massive transfusion protocol.

## Section 3: Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy (Questions 51-75)

### Subheading: Preeclampsia, Eclampsia, HELLP
**Q51: What defines Gestational Hypertension?**
A: BP >140/90 after 20 weeks without proteinuria or severe features.

**Q52: What defines Preeclampsia?**
A: BP >140/90 after 20 weeks PLUS proteinuria OR signs of end-organ damage.

**Q53: What are the severe features of preeclampsia?**
A: BP >160/110, thrombocytopenia, renal insufficiency, impaired liver function, pulmonary edema, cerebral/visual symptoms.

**Q54: What is the pathophysiology of preeclampsia?**
A: Abnormal placentation leading to endothelial dysfunction and systemic vasoconstriction.

**Q55: What is the definitive treatment for preeclampsia?**
A: Delivery of fetus and placenta.

**Q56: When should delivery occur in preeclampsia with severe features?**
A: At 34 weeks or sooner if maternal/fetal instability.

**Q57: What is the first-line medication for seizure prophylaxis in preeclampsia?**
A: Magnesium Sulfate.

**Q58: What is the loading and maintenance dose of Magnesium Sulfate?**
A: Loading: 4-6g IV over 20-30 mins; Maintenance: 2g/hr infusion.

**Q59: What are the signs of Magnesium toxicity?**
A: Loss of deep tendon reflexes (first), respiratory depression, cardiac arrest.

**Q60: What is the antidote for Magnesium toxicity?**
A: Calcium Gluconate (1g IV).

**Q61: What medications are used for acute BP control in preeclampsia?**
A: Labetalol, Hydralazine, Nifedipine.

**Q62: What BP threshold requires urgent treatment in pregnancy?**
A: Systolic >160 or Diastolic >110 sustained for 15 minutes.

**Q63: What is Eclampsia?**
A: New-onset generalized tonic-clonic seizures in a woman with preeclampsia.

**Q64: What is the immediate management of eclamptic seizure?**
A: Airway protection, Magnesium Sulfate bolus, prevent injury, deliver after stabilization.

**Q65: Why are Benzodiazepines not first-line for eclampsia?**
A: MgSO4 is superior for prevention/treatment of eclamptic seizures; benzos reserved for refractory cases.

**Q66: What is HELLP Syndrome?**
A: Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets; variant of severe preeclampsia.

**Q67: What are the lab criteria for HELLP?**
A: H: Schistocytes, elevated LDH/bilirubin; EL: AST/ALT >2x normal; LP: Platelets <100k.

**Q68: What is the classic presentation of HELLP?**
A: RUQ/epigastric pain, nausea/vomiting, malaise; may mimic hepatitis or gallbladder disease.

**Q69: What is the management of HELLP syndrome?**
A: Stabilization, Magnesium, BP control, prompt delivery.

**Q70: What hepatic complication can occur with HELLP?**
A: Subcapsular hepatic hematoma or rupture (severe RUQ pain, shock).

**Q71: What is the management of hepatic rupture in pregnancy?**
A: Emergent surgery/angiographic embolization; high mortality.

**Q72: Can preeclampsia occur postpartum?**
A: Yes, up to 4-6 weeks postpartum (postpartum preeclampsia).

**Q73: What are the long-term risks for women with history of preeclampsia?**
A: Increased risk of chronic hypertension, CVD, stroke, renal disease.

**Q74: What is the role of aspirin in preeclampsia prevention?**
A: Low-dose aspirin (81mg) starting at 12 weeks for high-risk women reduces incidence.

**Q75: Who is considered high-risk for preeclampsia?**
A: Prior preeclampsia, multifetal gestation, chronic HTN, diabetes, renal disease, autoimmune disease.

## Section 4: Labor & Delivery Emergencies (Questions 76-110)

### Subheading: Abnormal Labor & Delivery Complications
**Q76: What are the stages of labor?**
A: 1: Onset to full dilation; 2: Full dilation to delivery; 3: Delivery of placenta; 4: Recovery (1-2 hrs postpartum).

**Q77: What is Shoulder Dystocia?**
A: Failure of shoulders to deliver after head; obstetric emergency.

**Q78: What is the "Turtle Sign"?**
A: Retraction of delivered head against perineum; sign of shoulder dystocia.

**Q79: What is the first maneuver for shoulder dystocia?**
A: McRoberts maneuver (hyperflex thighs to abdomen) + Suprapubic pressure.

**Q80: What are subsequent maneuvers if McRoberts fails?**
A: Rubin's/Woods screw (rotate shoulders), delivery of posterior arm, all-fours maneuver.

**Q81: What is the Zavanelli maneuver?**
A: Cephalic replacement and C-section; last resort for shoulder dystocia.

**Q82: What are the complications of shoulder dystocia?**
A: Brachial plexus injury (Erb's/Klumpke), clavicle/humerus fracture, hypoxic brain injury.

**Q83: What is Umbilical Cord Prolapse?**
A: Cord descends below presenting part; risk of cord compression and fetal hypoxia.

**Q84: What are the risk factors for cord prolapse?**
A: Breech/transverse lie, polyhydramnios, prematurity, multiparity, amniotomy with high station.

**Q85: What is the immediate management of cord prolapse?**
A: Call for help, manual elevation of presenting part, knee-chest/Trendelenburg position, emergent C-section.

**Q86: Should you attempt to push prolapsed cord back in?**
A: No; handle minimally, keep moist, relieve pressure.

**Q87: What is Amniotic Fluid Embolism (AFE)?**
A: Anaphylactoid reaction to amniotic fluid entering maternal circulation; rare but fatal.

**Q88: What is the classic triad of AFE?**
A: Hypoxia/respiratory distress, hypotension/shock, coagulopathy (DIC).

**Q89: When does AFE typically occur?**
A: During labor, delivery, or immediately postpartum.

**Q90: What is the management of AFE?**
A: Supportive care (ABCs, vasopressors, blood products); no specific antidote.

**Q91: What is Precipitous Labor?**
A: Delivery within <3 hours of onset of contractions.

**Q92: What are the risks of precipitous labor?**
A: Lacerations, PPH, intracranial hemorrhage in infant, unsterile delivery.

**Q93: What is Occiput Posterior (OP) position?**
A: "Sunny side up"; associated with back labor, prolonged second stage.

**Q94: What is Brow/Face presentation?**
A: Deflexed head; brow usually requires C-section, face may deliver vaginally if mentum anterior.

**Q95: What is Breech presentation?**
A: Buttocks/feet first; Frank, Complete, or Footling.

**Q96: What is the management of footling breech in labor?**
A: C-section (high risk of cord prolapse).

**Q97: What is External Cephalic Version (ECV)?**
A: Manual turning of fetus to vertex; offered at 37 weeks for breech.

**Q98: What are contraindications to ECV?**
A: Placenta previa, abruption, non-reassuring fetal status, uterine anomalies, multiples.

**Q99: What is Uterine Inversion?**
A: Fundus collapses into cavity; rare, life-threatening.

**Q100: What causes uterine inversion?**
A: Excessive traction on cord, fundal pressure during third stage.

**Q101: What is the management of uterine inversion?**
A: Immediate manual replacement (Johnson maneuver), stop uterotonics, tocolytics if needed, surgery if failed.

**Q102: What is the O'Sullivan hydrostatic method?**
A: Using warm saline infusion into vagina to push inverted uterus back.

**Q103: What is a Nuchal Cord?**
A: Cord wrapped around neck; common, usually benign.

**Q104: How do you manage nuchal cord at delivery?**
A: Slip over head if loose; clamp/cut if tight (somersault maneuver alternative).

**Q105: What is Meconium Stained Amniotic Fluid (MSAF)?**
A: Green/brown fluid; sign of fetal stress or post-maturity.

**Q106: What is the management of MSAF?**
A: Suction mouth/nose only if baby is NOT vigorous; otherwise routine care. Intubation for suction no longer routine.

**Q107: What defines a "vigorous" newborn with MSAF?**
A: Strong cry, good tone, HR >100.

**Q108: What is Chorioamnionitis?**
A: Intra-amniotic infection; fever, uterine tenderness, foul lochia, maternal/fetal tachycardia.

**Q109: What is the management of chorioamnionitis?**
A: Broad-spectrum antibiotics (Ampicillin+Gentamicin), expedite delivery.

**Q110: What are the neonatal risks of chorioamnionitis?**
A: Sepsis, pneumonia, meningitis.

## Section 5: Postpartum Hemorrhage (Questions 111-135)

### Subheading: Causes & Management of PPH
**Q111: What is the definition of Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH)?**
A: Blood loss >1000mL or any amount with hemodynamic instability (within 24h = early; >24h = late).

**Q112: What are the 4 T's of PPH?**
A: Tone (atony), Trauma (lacerations), Tissue (retained placenta), Thrombin (coagulopathy).

**Q113: What is the most common cause of PPH?**
A: Uterine Atony (Tone).

**Q114: What are risk factors for uterine atony?**
A: Overdistension (twins/polyhydramnios), prolonged labor, multiparity, chorioamnionitis, magnesium use.

**Q115: What is the first step in managing uterine atony?**
A: Bimanual uterine massage.

**Q116: What is the first-line uterotonic drug?**
A: Oxytocin (Pitocin).

**Q117: What second-line agents are used for atony?**
A: Methylergonovine (Methergine), Carboprost (Hemabate), Misoprostol.

**Q118: What is the contraindication for Methylergonovine?**
A: Hypertension/preeclampsia (causes vasoconstriction).

**Q119: What is the contraindication for Carboprost?**
A: Asthma (can cause bronchospasm).

**Q120: What is the mechanism of Tranexamic Acid (TXA) in PPH?**
A: Antifibrinolytic; reduces mortality if given within 3 hours.

**Q121: What is the dose of TXA for PPH?**
A: 1g IV over 10 mins.

**Q122: How do you assess for genital tract trauma?**
A: Systematic inspection of cervix, vagina, perineum under good lighting.

**Q123: What is a Vulvar/Vaginal Hematoma?**
A: Collection of blood in soft tissue; presents with pain, pressure, discoloration.

**Q124: What is the management of expanding hematoma?**
A: Incision, evacuation, ligation of bleeding vessel, packing.

**Q125: What suggests retained placental tissue?**
A: Boggy uterus, continued bleeding, missing cotyledons on inspection of placenta.

**Q126: How is retained placenta managed?**
A: Manual removal, D&C, or vacuum aspiration.

**Q127: What is Placenta Accreta Spectrum?**
A: Abnormal invasion of placenta into myometrium (Accreta, Increta, Percreta).

**Q128: What are risk factors for accreta?**
A: Prior C-section + placenta previa (risk increases with number of C-sections).

**Q129: What is the management of accreta?**
A: Planned C-section hysterectomy; DO NOT attempt manual removal (massive hemorrhage).

**Q130: What is Late PPH?**
A: Bleeding 24h to 12 weeks postpartum; often due to retained tissue or subinvolution.

**Q131: What is Subinvolution of the uterus?**
A: Uterus fails to contract down; often due to infection or retained tissue.

**Q132: What is the role of balloon tamponade in PPH?**
A: Mechanical compression of uterine cavity (Bakri balloon) for refractory atony.

**Q133: When is surgical intervention indicated for PPH?**
A: Failure of medical management; options include B-Lynch suture, artery ligation, hysterectomy.

**Q134: What is the role of Interventional Radiology in PPH?**
A: Uterine artery embolization for stable patients with ongoing bleeding.

**Q135: What is Sheehan's Syndrome?**
A: Postpartum pituitary necrosis due to severe hypotension/PPH; presents with failure to lactate, amenorrhea.

## Section 6: Fetal Monitoring & Distress (Questions 136-155)

### Subheading: Interpretation & Interventions
**Q136: What are the three categories of Fetal Heart Rate (FHR) tracings?**
A: Category I (Normal), II (Indeterminate), III (Abnormal).

**Q137: What defines a Category I tracing?**
A: Baseline 110-160, moderate variability, no late/variable decels, accelerations present.

**Q138: What defines a Category III tracing?**
A: Absent variability + recurrent late/variable decels OR sinusoidal pattern.

**Q139: What is Fetal Bradycardia?**
A: Baseline <110 bpm; concerning if prolonged.

**Q140: What is Fetal Tachycardia?**
A: Baseline >160 bpm; causes include maternal fever, drugs, fetal anemia, arrhythmia.

**Q141: What is Variability?**
A: Fluctuations in baseline FHR; indicates intact CNS/oxygenation.

**Q142: What is the significance of absent variability?**
A: Fetal sleep cycle, drugs, acidosis, CNS anomaly.

**Q143: What are Early Decelerations?**
A: Mirror image of contraction; head compression; benign.

**Q144: What are Late Decelerations?**
A: Start after peak of contraction; uteroplacental insufficiency; concerning.

**Q145: What are Variable Decelerations?**
A: Abrupt drop in FHR; cord compression; common.

**Q146: When are variable decelerations concerning?**
A: If prolonged, severe (<70bpm), or with slow return to baseline.

**Q147: What is a Sinusoidal Pattern?**
A: Smooth wave-like pattern; suggests severe fetal anemia or acidosis.

**Q148: What is the initial management of non-reassuring FHR?**
A: Intrauterine Resuscitation: Position change (left lateral), O2, IV fluids, stop oxytocin, treat hypotension.

**Q149: What is Terbutaline used for in labor?**
A: Tocolysis to relax uterus for fetal resuscitation or internal version.

**Q150: What is Amnioinfusion?**
A: Infusion of saline into uterus; treats recurrent variable decels (cord compression).

**Q151: What is Scalp Stimulation?**
A: Digital stimulation of fetal scalp; tests for acidosis (should cause acceleration).

**Q152: What is Fetal Scalp pH sampling?**
A: Direct measurement of fetal pH; <7.20 suggests acidosis.

**Q153: When is emergent delivery indicated for fetal distress?**
A: Category III tracing, prolonged bradycardia, failure of resuscitation.

**Q154: What is the decision-to-delivery interval for emergent C-section?**
A: Ideally <30 minutes for true emergencies.

**Q155: What is the role of fetal pulse oximetry?**
A: Limited use; not routinely recommended.

## Section 7: Medical Emergencies in Pregnancy (Questions 156-175)

**Q156: How does pregnancy affect airway management?**
A: Edema, weight gain, breast enlargement make intubation difficult; rapid desaturation (decreased FRC).

**Q157: What is the preferred positioning for intubation in pregnant patients?**
A: Ramp position (head elevated) to offset aortic compression and improve view.

**Q158: What is the risk of aspiration in pregnancy?**
A: Increased (delayed gastric emptying, decreased LES tone); RSI with cricoid pressure recommended.

**Q159: What is Pulmonary Embolism risk in pregnancy?**
A: 5x increased risk; hypercoagulable state.

**Q160: How is PE diagnosed in pregnancy?**
A: CTA chest (low fetal radiation) or V/Q scan; D-dimer less useful (elevated normally).

**Q161: What is the treatment for PE in pregnancy?**
A: LMWH (Enoxaparin) or Unfractionated Heparin; Warfarin contraindicated (teratogenic).

**Q162: What is Acute Fatty Liver of Pregnancy (AFLP)?**
A: Rare, life-threatening; third trimester liver failure (hypoglycemia, coagulopathy, encephalopathy).

**Q163: How does AFLP differ from HELLP?**
A: AFLP has severe hypoglycemia, coagulopathy, renal failure; requires immediate delivery.

**Q164: What is Cholestasis of Pregnancy?**
A: Pruritus (palms/soles), elevated bile acids; risk of stillbirth.

**Q165: What is the treatment for Cholestasis?**
A: Ursodeoxycholic acid, early delivery (37wks).

**Q166: What appendicitis presentation changes in pregnancy?**
A: Pain migrates higher (RUQ in third trimester) due to displaced appendix.

**Q167: What is the imaging of choice for appendicitis in pregnancy?**
A: Ultrasound first, then MRI; CT if necessary (risk/benefit).

**Q168: What is Pyelonephritis risk in pregnancy?**
A: Increased due to ureteral dilation; treat aggressively (IV abx) to prevent sepsis/preterm labor.

**Q169: What antibiotics are safe in pregnancy?**
A: Penicillins, Cephalosporins, Erythromycin. Avoid: Fluoroquinolones, Tetracyclines, Aminoglycosides (relative).

**Q170: What is the management of Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) in pregnancy?**
A: Similar to non-pregnant (fluids, insulin, K+); lower threshold for ICU, continuous fetal monitoring.

**Q171: Why is DKA more dangerous in pregnancy?**
A: Occurs at lower glucose levels, higher risk of fetal loss, preterm labor.

**Q172: What is Thyroid Storm in pregnancy?**
A: Hyperthyroid crisis; fever, tachycardia, agitation.

**Q173: What is the treatment for Thyroid Storm?**
A: PTU (first trimester) or Methimazole, Beta-blockers, Steroids, Iodine (after antithyroid meds).

**Q174: What is Myasthenia Gravis effect on pregnancy?**
A: Variable course; neonatal transient MG possible.

**Q175: What medications are teratogenic?**
A: ACE inhibitors, Warfarin, Valproate, Methotrexate, Isotretinoin, Thalidomide.

## Section 8: Trauma in Pregnancy (Questions 176-185)

**Q176: What is the leading cause of fetal death in trauma?**
A: Maternal shock/hypoxia; also placental abruption.

**Q177: How does physiologic change affect trauma assessment?**
A: Increased blood volume masks shock (tachycardia may be only sign); uterus displaces organs.

**Q178: What is the management of pregnant trauma patient?**
A: ATLS protocols + Left Lateral Tilt (prevent aortocaval compression), fetal monitoring.

**Q179: When should fetal monitoring be initiated after trauma?**
A: All viable pregnancies (>20-24wks) for minimum 4-6 hours.

**Q180: What is the Kleihauer-Betke test indication in trauma?**
A: All Rh-negative trauma patients to determine Rhogam dosing.

**Q181: What is the threshold for Rhogam in trauma?**
A: Any significant trauma; standard dose 300mcg covers 30mL fetomaternal bleed.

**Q182: What is Perimortem C-section?**
A: Resuscitative hysterotomy within 4-5 minutes of maternal cardiac arrest.

**Q183: What is the goal of Perimortem C-section?**
A: Improve maternal venous return (aortocaval decompression) AND save fetus if viable (>24wks).

**Q184: Where is the incision for Perimortem C-section?**
A: Vertical midline (fastest access).

**Q185: Do you need consent for Perimortem C-section?**
A: Implied consent in cardiac arrest; proceed immediately.

## Section 9: Toxicology & Special Topics (Questions 186-200)

**Q186: What is the management of acetaminophen overdose in pregnancy?**
A: Same as non-pregnant; NAC is safe and crosses placenta (treats fetus too).

**Q187: What is the concern with Carbon Monoxide in pregnancy?**
A: Fetal Hb binds CO tighter; fetal levels higher than maternal.

**Q188: What is the threshold for Hyperbaric Oxygen in pregnant CO poisoning?**
A: Lower threshold; consider if maternal levels >20% or any fetal distress.

**Q189: Is alcohol safe in pregnancy?**
A: No; Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders; no known safe limit.

**Q190: What is the effect of cocaine in pregnancy?**
A: Vasoconstriction → abruption, preterm labor, IUGR.

**Q191: What is Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome (NAS)?**
A: Withdrawal in newborn from maternal opioid use; tremors, irritability, feeding issues.

**Q192: What is the treatment for Opioid Use Disorder in pregnancy?**
A: Methadone or Buprenorphine maintenance; detox not recommended (relapse risk).

**Q193: What is Group B Strep (GBS) prophylaxis?**
A: Penicillin during labor for colonized mothers to prevent neonatal sepsis.

**Q194: When is GBS screening done?**
A: 35-37 weeks gestation.

**Q195: What if GBS status unknown in labor?**
A: Treat if risk factors: preterm, ROM >18h, fever, prior GBS baby.

**Q196: What is Cerclage?**
A: Stitch to close cervix; for cervical insufficiency.

**Q197: When is Cerclage removed?**
A: 36-37 weeks or if labor starts.

**Q198: What is Tocolysis?**
A: Medications to stop contractions (Nifedipine, Indomethacin, Terbutaline).

**Q199: What are indications for Tocolysis?**
A: Preterm labor (<34wks) to allow steroids/magnesium transfer or transport.

**Q200: What are contraindications to Tocolysis?**
A: Chorioamnionitis, severe preeclampsia, abruption, fetal distress, lethal anomaly.
