# Master Case: Preeclampsia & Eclampsia
## Clinical Timeline-Based Narrative: The Sudden Headache

### Triage (14:00)
A 32-year-old G1P0 at 34 weeks and 2 days gestation presents to the Emergency Department complaining of a "blinding" frontal headache for 2 hours and new-onset blurry vision. She notes that her feet and hands have become very swollen over the last 48 hours.

### Evaluation (14:15)
- **Initial BP:** 172/114 mmHg
- **Pulse:** 88 bpm
- **Appearance:** Distressed
- **Physical Exam:** 3+ pitting edema to the knees, 4+ patellar reflexes with 2 beats of clonus.
- **Fundoscopy:** Mild retinal arteriolar narrowing.
- **Bedside Urine Dipstick:** 3+ proteinuria.

### Diagnostics (15:00)
**Laboratory Results:**
- Platelets: 95,000/μL
- AST: 110 U/L, ALT: 125 U/L
- LDH: 750 U/L
- Creatinine: 1.2 mg/dL (baseline 0.6)
- Protein/Creatinine Ratio (PCR): 0.85 mg/mg

### Clinical Decision (15:30)
The diagnosis of **Preeclampsia with Severe Features** (including HELLP syndrome components) is made. The patient is at high risk for eclampsia.

### Acute Management (15:45)
- **Magnesium Sulfate:** Loading dose 4g IV, followed by maintenance infusion 2g/hr.
- **Antihypertensive:** Labetalol 20mg IV bolus given to address hypertensive crisis.

### Crisis (16:30)
While being monitored, the patient begins a generalized tonic-clonic seizure. Airway is protected, and a 2g IV Magnesium bolus is prepared for recurrent seizure activity.

### Definitive Care (18:00)
Following stabilization, the decision is made for urgent delivery via Cesarean section due to eclampsia and deteriorating maternal status at 34 weeks.

## 100 Rapid-Fire Clinical Questions & Answers

### Triage & Initial Assessment (Q1-Q15)

**Q1: What is the definition of hypertension in pregnancy?**
A: Systolic BP ≥140 mmHg or Diastolic BP ≥90 mmHg on two occasions at least 4 hours apart.

**Q2: What is "Severe Hypertension"?**
A: Systolic BP ≥160 mmHg or Diastolic BP ≥110 mmHg.

**Q3: Define Preeclampsia.**
A: New-onset hypertension after 20 weeks gestation PLUS proteinuria OR evidence of end-organ dysfunction.

**Q4: What is the gold standard for diagnosing proteinuria?**
A: 24-hour urine collection (≥300 mg) or Protein/Creatinine Ratio (≥0.3 mg/mg or 30 mg/mmol).

**Q5: Is edema still a diagnostic criterion for preeclampsia?**
A: No, as it is too common in normal pregnancy, but sudden/rapid onset is clinically significant.

**Q6: What are the "Severe Features" of preeclampsia?**
A: Severe HTN, thrombocytopenia, impaired liver function, progressive renal insufficiency, pulmonary edema, or new-onset cerebral/visual disturbances.

**Q7: Why is hyperreflexia significant?**
A: It indicates neuromuscular irritability and increased risk of seizures (eclampsia).

**Q8: What is "Clonus"?**
A: A series of involuntary, rhythmic muscular contractions and relaxations, usually elicited at the ankle; indicates upper motor neuron irritability.

**Q9: What is the significance of RUQ or epigastric pain?**
A: Suggests hepatic capsule stretch (Glisson's capsule) due to edema or hemorrhage, seen in HELLP or severe preeclampsia.

**Q10: Can preeclampsia occur before 20 weeks?**
A: Rarely, and if it does, it should prompt investigation for molar pregnancy or antiphospholipid syndrome.

**Q11: What is the most common risk factor for preeclampsia?**
A: Nulliparity.

**Q12: Name three high-risk factors that warrant Aspirin prophylaxis.**
A: Hypertensive disease in previous pregnancy, chronic kidney disease, autoimmune disease (SLE/APS), Type 1 or 2 diabetes, or chronic hypertension.

**Q13: What is the recommended dose and timing for Aspirin prophylaxis?**
A: 75–150 mg daily (ASPRE trial suggests 150mg) starting from 12 weeks until delivery.

**Q14: How does Chronic HTN differ from Gestational HTN?**
A: Chronic HTN is present before pregnancy or before 20 weeks; Gestational HTN develops after 20 weeks without proteinuria/end-organ damage.

**Q15: What is "Superimposed Preeclampsia"?**
A: Preeclampsia developing in a patient with pre-existing chronic hypertension.

### Diagnostic Decision Making (Q16-Q35)

**Q16: What does the acronym HELLP stand for?**
A: Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets.

**Q17: What are the laboratory criteria for HELLP (Tennessee Criteria)?**
A: LDH >600 U/L, AST ≥70 U/L, and Platelets <100,000/μL.

**Q18: What is the significance of Schistocytes on a peripheral smear?**
A: Evidence of microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (MAHA).

**Q19: What happens to Haptoglobin in HELLP?**
A: It decreases (it binds to free hemoglobin released during hemolysis).

**Q20: What is the "sFlt-1/PlGF ratio"?**
A: A biomarker test used to predict the development of preeclampsia (low PlGF and high sFlt-1 suggest placental dysfunction).

**Q21: According to the PROGNOSIS study, what ratio of sFlt-1/PlGF helps rule out preeclampsia for 1 week?**
A: A ratio ≤38.

**Q22: Why is Uric Acid sometimes measured?**
A: Hyperuricemia correlates with the severity of renal involvement and placental oxidative stress, though not diagnostic.

**Q23: How does preeclampsia affect the fetus?**
A: Fetal growth restriction (FGR), oligohydramnios, and increased risk of placental abruption.

**Q24: What is the primary pathophysiology of preeclampsia?**
A: Defective spiral artery remodeling leading to placental ischemia and systemic endothelial dysfunction.

**Q25: What is the target BP in preeclampsia without severe features?**
A: Aim for <140/90 mmHg (NICE) or 135/85 mmHg.

**Q26: Is Preeclampsia with Severe Features an indication for immediate delivery at 32 weeks?**
A: Often yes, after maternal stabilization and steroid administration, unless "expectant management" is pursued in a tertiary center.

**Q27: Define "Impaired Liver Function" in preeclampsia.**
A: Liver transaminases at least twice the upper limit of normal.

**Q28: Define "Renal Insufficiency" in preeclampsia.**
A: Serum creatinine >1.1 mg/dL or doubling of baseline in the absence of other renal disease.

**Q29: What is the threshold for thrombocytopenia?**
A: Platelets <100,000/μL.

**Q30: What visual symptoms are most common?**
A: Scotomata (blind spots), photopsia (flashing lights), or blurred vision.

**Q31: What is the risk of stroke in preeclampsia?**
A: High, especially if Systolic BP stays >160 mmHg (hemorrhagic stroke).

**Q32: Can preeclampsia present for the first time postpartum?**
A: Yes, up to 6 weeks postpartum (Postpartum Preeclampsia).

**Q33: What is "Early-onset" vs. "Late-onset" preeclampsia?**
A: Early-onset is <34 weeks (more associated with placental dysfunction); Late-onset is ≥34 weeks.

**Q34: Does proteinuria level correlate with maternal or fetal outcomes?**
A: Not linearly; once proteinuria is confirmed, repeated 24-hour collections do not guide management.

**Q35: What is the role of Doppler ultrasound in management?**
A: Umbilical artery Doppler assesses placental resistance; Uterine artery Doppler in the second trimester screens for risk.

### Acute ED Management (Q36-Q55)

**Q36: What is the first-line IV agent for hypertensive crisis in pregnancy?**
A: Labetalol or Hydralazine.

**Q37: What is the dose for IV Labetalol bolus?**
A: Start with 20mg, then 40mg, then 80mg every 10-15 mins (Max 300mg).

**Q38: What are the contraindications to Labetalol?**
A: Asthma, heart failure, or heart block.

**Q39: What is the dose for IV Hydralazine?**
A: 5-10 mg IV every 20 mins.

**Q40: What is a common side effect of Hydralazine?**
A: Reflex tachycardia and headaches.

**Q41: Can Nifedipine be used acutely?**
A: Yes, oral immediate-release (10-20mg) is effective if IV access is not yet established.

**Q42: What is the "Magnesium Sulfate" loading dose?**
A: 4g to 6g IV over 15-20 minutes.

**Q43: What is the maintenance dose for Magnesium?**
A: 1g to 2g per hour.

**Q44: Why is Magnesium Sulfate used?**
A: For seizure prophylaxis (neuroprotection); it is not an antihypertensive.

**Q45: What is the "Magpie Trial" finding?**
A: Magnesium Sulfate reduces the risk of eclampsia by more than 50% in women with preeclampsia.

**Q46: Should fluids be restricted or pushed in preeclampsia?**
A: Restricted (usually <80-100 mL/hr total) to prevent pulmonary edema, as these patients have "leaky" capillaries.

**Q47: When should steroids (Betamethasone) be given?**
A: If delivery is anticipated <37 weeks (ideally <34 weeks) for fetal lung maturity.

**Q48: What is the dose of Betamethasone?**
A: 12 mg IM, two doses 24 hours apart.

**Q49: Can Magnesium and Nifedipine be used together?**
A: Yes, but monitor for potential synergistic effect causing hypotension or neuromuscular blockade (rare).

**Q50: What is the management of "Severe Hypertension" that is refractory to first-line agents?**
A: Consider Nicardipine infusion or Sodium Nitroprusside (very short term only).

**Q51: Is there a role for Loop Diuretics?**
A: Only in cases of confirmed pulmonary edema or heart failure.

**Q52: What is the target BP during acute management of severe HTN?**
A: 140-150 / 90-100 mmHg (avoid dropping too fast to protect placental perfusion).

**Q53: What is the "Dose-Response" for IV Labetalol?**
A: It works within 5-10 minutes and lasts 3-6 hours.

**Q54: What is the most common error in BP measurement?**
A: Using a cuff that is too small for the patient's arm.

**Q55: How should BP be measured?**
A: Patient sitting or semi-reclined, arm at heart level, using a calibrated device.

### Eclampsia Management (Q56-Q75)

**Q56: Define Eclampsia.**
A: New-onset generalized tonic-clonic seizures in a patient with preeclampsia.

**Q57: What is the first priority during an eclamptic seizure?**
A: Call for help and protect the airway (left lateral position, suction, oxygen).

**Q58: Does an eclamptic seizure require an immediate C-section during the fit?**
A: No. Stabilize the mother first. Bradycardia in the fetus is common during the seizure and usually recovers after maternal stabilization.

**Q59: How do you treat a recurrent seizure while on Magnesium?**
A: Give a further 2g IV bolus of Magnesium Sulfate.

**Q60: Are Diazepam or Phenytoin first-line for eclampsia?**
A: No. The Magpie trial and Collaborative Eclampsia Trial showed Magnesium is superior to both.

**Q61: When should you consider Midazolam or Thiopental?**
A: Only for status epilepticus or if Magnesium is contraindicated/unavailable.

**Q62: What is the duration of a typical eclamptic seizure?**
A: 60-90 seconds.

**Q63: What is the most common time for eclampsia to occur?**
A: Intrapartum or within 48 hours postpartum (though can be antepartum).

**Q64: What is the "Postictal State"?**
A: The period of altered consciousness or somnolence following a seizure.

**Q65: How does Magnesium work as an anticonvulsant?**
A: NMDA receptor antagonism and cerebral vasodilation.

**Q66: Should you give Magnesium to all women with "Gestational HTN"?**
A: No, only those with "Severe Features" or "Severe Preeclampsia."

**Q67: What are the warning signs of impending eclampsia?**
A: Severe headache, visual changes, RUQ pain, and hyperreflexia.

**Q68: What is the differential diagnosis for eclampsia?**
A: Epilepsy, stroke, intracranial hemorrhage, meningitis, or cerebral venous thrombosis.

**Q69: When is neuroimaging (CT/MRI) indicated in eclampsia?**
A: Atypical presentation (e.g., focal deficits, prolonged coma, seizure despite therapeutic magnesium, or onset >48h postpartum).

**Q70: What is PRES?**
A: Posterior Reversible Encephalopathy Syndrome; a radiographic finding often seen in eclampsia.

**Q71: How long should Magnesium be continued after delivery?**
A: Usually 24 hours after delivery or 24 hours after the last seizure.

**Q72: Can a patient eat during a Magnesium infusion?**
A: Usually NPO or clear liquids due to risk of aspiration and potential surgery.

**Q73: What is the role of Oxygen?**
A: 8-10 L/min via non-rebreather mask during and after a seizure.

**Q74: Is "biting the tongue" common in eclampsia?**
A: Yes, it can occur during the tonic phase.

**Q75: What is the most common maternal cause of death in eclampsia?**
A: Intracranial hemorrhage.

### Complication Management (Q76-Q90)

**Q76: What are the signs of Magnesium Toxicity?**
A: Loss of deep tendon reflexes (first sign), respiratory depression, and cardiac arrest.

**Q77: What is the antidote for Magnesium Toxicity?**
A: Calcium Gluconate (1g IV over 5-10 mins).

**Q78: What is the therapeutic range for serum Magnesium?**
A: 4-7 mEq/L (4.8–8.4 mg/dL).

**Q79: At what level does loss of patellar reflexes usually occur?**
A: 7-10 mEq/L.

**Q80: At what level does respiratory paralysis occur?**
A: 10-13 mEq/L.

**Q81: Why is monitoring urine output critical during Mag infusion?**
A: Magnesium is almost exclusively cleared by the kidneys. Low UOP leads to rapid toxicity.

**Q82: What is the threshold for "Low Urine Output"?**
A: <30 mL/hr or <0.5 mL/kg/hr.

**Q83: What is the risk of "Placental Abruption" in preeclampsia?**
A: Significantly increased due to placental vascular friability.

**Q84: What is the risk of "Pulmonary Edema"?**
A: High (approx. 2-5%), often occurs postpartum due to fluid mobilization.

**Q85: How do you treat HELLP syndrome?**
A: Delivery is the only definitive treatment.

**Q86: Is there a role for Dexamethasone in HELLP?**
A: Controversial; it may improve platelet counts but does not improve maternal or fetal outcomes (COBALT trial).

**Q87: When is Platelet Transfusion indicated in HELLP?**
A: If platelets <20,000/μL for vaginal delivery or <50,000/μL for C-section.

**Q88: What is the risk of "Liver Rupture"?**
A: Rare but catastrophic; presents with sudden shock and RUQ pain.

**Q89: How does preeclampsia affect long-term cardiovascular health?**
A: Increases risk of future HTN, stroke, and ischemic heart disease (2-fold increase).

**Q90: What is the "Fishberg Test"?**
A: An old test for renal concentration; no longer used.

### Disposition & Prognosis (Q91-Q100)

**Q91: When should a patient with "Gestational HTN" be delivered?**
A: 37 weeks 0 days.

**Q92: When should a patient with "Preeclampsia without Severe Features" be delivered?**
A: 37 weeks 0 days (HYPITAT trial).

**Q93: When should a patient with "Preeclampsia with Severe Features" be delivered?**
A: At or after 34 weeks 0 days.

**Q94: If Preeclampsia with Severe Features occurs <34 weeks, what is the approach?**
A: Maternal stabilization, steroids, and expectant management in a specialized unit if both mother and fetus are stable.

**Q95: Can a patient with HELLP have an Epidural?**
A: Generally avoided if Platelets <70,000-80,000/μL due to risk of spinal hematoma.

**Q96: What is the recurrence risk of preeclampsia?**
A: 15-20% overall (higher if early-onset).

**Q97: What follow-up is needed postpartum?**
A: BP monitoring for at least 5-7 days; PCP follow-up at 1 week and 6 weeks.

**Q98: When can antihypertensives be tapered?**
A: Once BP is consistently <140/90 mmHg, usually weeks to months postpartum.

**Q99: Should NSAIDs be used for postpartum pain in preeclampsia?**
A: Some guidelines suggest caution or avoidance as they can theoretically worsen HTN/renal function, but clinical evidence is mixed.

**Q100: What advice should be given for future pregnancies?**
A: Early booking, Aspirin from 12 weeks, and weight/lifestyle optimization.
