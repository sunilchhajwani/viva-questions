# Master Case: Pediatric Emergencies

## Section 1: Pediatric Assessment & Resuscitation

**Q1: What is the Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT)?**
A: Appearance, Work of Breathing, Circulation to Skin - rapid hands-off assessment.

**Q2: What are normal heart rate ranges by age?**
A: Infant (0-1yr): 100-160, Toddler (1-3yr): 90-150, Preschool (3-5yr): 80-140, School (5-12yr): 70-120, Adolescent: 60-100.

**Q3: What are normal systolic BP lower limits by age?**
A: Infant: >70 mmHg, Child 1-10yr: >70 + (2 x age in years), Child >10yr: >90 mmHg.

**Q4: What is the most common cause of cardiac arrest in children?**
A: Respiratory failure/hypoxia (unlike adults where it's cardiac).

**Q5: What compression-to-ventilation ratio for pediatric CPR?**
A: Single rescuer: 30:2, Two rescuers (healthcare): 15:2.

**Q6: What is the correct compression depth for children?**
A: At least 1/3 AP diameter of chest (~5cm infant, ~5cm child).

**Q7: How do you calculate ET tube size for children?**
A: Uncuffed: (age/4) + 4, Cuffed: (age/4) + 3.5.

**Q8: What is the Broselow tape used for?**
A: Color-coded length-based resuscitation tool for weight estimation, drug dosing, equipment sizing.

**Q9: What are signs of compensated shock in children?**
A: Tachycardia, delayed capillary refill (>2sec), cool extremities, normal BP (children maintain BP until late).

**Q10: What fluid bolus for pediatric shock?**
A: 20 mL/kg isotonic crystalloid (NS or LR), repeat as needed.

## Section 2: Pediatric Respiratory Emergencies

**Q11: What is the classic presentation of croup?**
A: Barking cough, inspiratory stridor, hoarse voice, often preceded by URI symptoms.

**Q12: How do you treat moderate-severe croup?**
A: Nebulized epinephrine (racemic 0.5mL or L-epi 5mL), dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg PO/IM/IV.

**Q13: What is the Westley croup score?**
A: Assesses stridor, retractions, air entry, cyanosis, consciousness to grade severity.

**Q14: What organism causes epiglottitis?**
A: Haemophilus influenzae type B (now rare due to vaccination).

**Q15: What is the classic position in epiglottitis?**
A: Tripod position, drooling, distressed, "hot potato" voice.

**Q16: How do you manage suspected epiglottitis?**
A: Do NOT examine throat, keep child calm, immediate OR for intubation, IV antibiotics.

**Q17: What is bronchiolitis most commonly caused by?**
A: Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV).

**Q18: How do you treat bronchiolitis?**
A: Supportive care only: nasal suctioning, hydration, oxygen if hypoxic. No bronchodilators or steroids.

**Q19: When do you admit bronchiolitis patients?**
A: Hypoxia (<90%), poor feeding/dehydration, apnea, age <3 months, comorbidities.

**Q20: What is the classic triad of asthma exacerbation?**
A: Wheezing, cough, dyspnea with prolonged expiratory phase.

## Section 3: Pediatric Asthma Management

**Q21: What is first-line treatment for acute asthma?**
A: Inhaled SABA (albuterol) via MDI with spacer or nebulizer.

**Q22: What is the dose of albuterol for children?**
A: 2.5-5 mg nebulized q20min x3, then q1-4h PRN.

**Q23: When do you add ipratropium in asthma?**
A: Moderate-severe exacerbations (first 3 doses alongside albuterol).

**Q24: What steroid dose for acute asthma?**
A: Prednisolone 1-2 mg/kg (max 60mg) or dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg.

**Q25: What are indications for IV magnesium in asthma?**
A: Severe exacerbation unresponsive to initial therapy.

**Q26: What is the magnesium sulfate dose?**
A: 50 mg/kg IV (max 2g) over 20 minutes.

**Q27: When do you intubate asthmatic children?**
A: Respiratory failure, altered mental status, exhaustion, rising CO2 despite treatment.

**Q28: What ventilator settings for severe asthma?**
A: Low rate, long expiratory time, permissive hypercapnia, avoid high pressures.

**Q29: What is a silent chest in asthma?**
A: Absent breath sounds due to minimal air movement - ominous sign of impending arrest.

**Q30: What differentiates asthma from foreign body aspiration?**
A: Sudden onset, focal wheeze, history of choking, unilateral decreased air entry.

## Section 4: Pediatric Cardiac Emergencies

**Q31: What is the most common congenital heart defect?**
A: Ventricular septal defect (VSD).

**Q32: What are cyanotic heart defects (5 T's)?**
A: Tetralogy of Fallot, Transposition of great vessels, Truncus arteriosus, Tricuspid atresia, Total anomalous pulmonary venous return.

**Q33: How does Tetralogy of Fallot present?**
A: Cyanosis, squatting (increases SVR), harsh systolic murmur, "tet spells."

**Q34: How do you manage a tet spell?**
A: Knee-chest position, oxygen, morphine, fluids, phenylephrine (increase SVR).

**Q35: What is the ductal-dependent lesion management?**
A: Prostaglandin E1 infusion to keep PDA open (0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/min).

**Q36: What are side effects of prostaglandin E1?**
A: Apnea (be prepared to intubate), flushing, hypotension, fever.

**Q37: What is SVT in infants?**
A: HR >220 bpm, narrow complex, no visible P waves, sudden onset.

**Q38: How do you treat stable SVT in children?**
A: Vagal maneuvers (ice to face in infants), adenosine 0.1 mg/kg (max 6mg), may repeat 0.2 mg/kg.

**Q39: What energy for pediatric cardioversion?**
A: 0.5-1 J/kg initially, then 2 J/kg.

**Q40: What energy for pediatric defibrillation?**
A: 2 J/kg, then 4 J/kg, then ≥4 J/kg (max adult dose).

## Section 5: Pediatric Neurological Emergencies

**Q41: What is the most common cause of seizures in children 6mo-5yr?**
A: Febrile seizures.

**Q42: How do you define simple febrile seizure?**
A: Generalized, <15 min, single in 24hrs, age 6mo-5yr, returns to baseline.

**Q43: Do simple febrile seizures need workup?**
A: No EEG or neuroimaging needed if classic presentation, focus on source of fever.

**Q44: How do you treat active seizures in children?**
A: Benzodiazepines: lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg or midazolam 0.2 mg/kg IM/IN/IV.

**Q45: What is status epilepticus definition?**
A: Seizure >5 min or recurrent without return to baseline.

**Q46: What is second-line for status epilepticus?**
A: Fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg, levetiracetam 60 mg/kg, or phenobarbital 20 mg/kg.

**Q47: What is the most common cause of altered mental status in children?**
A: Infection, intoxication, metabolic, trauma.

**Q48: What glucose level defines hypoglycemia in neonates?**
A: <45 mg/dL (term), <40 mg/dL (preterm).

**Q49: What glucose level defines hypoglycemia in older children?**
A: <60 mg/dL.

**Q50: What is D10 dose for pediatric hypoglycemia?**
A: 2-4 mL/kg of D10W (provides 200-400 mg/kg glucose).
