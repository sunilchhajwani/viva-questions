# Pediatric Emergency Medicine: Comprehensive Viva Questions (200 Q&A)

## Section 1: Pediatric Resuscitation & Assessment (Questions 1-20)

### Subheading: Pediatric Assessment Triangle & Primary Survey
**Q1: What are the three components of the Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT)?**
A: Appearance, Work of Breathing, and Circulation to Skin.

**Q2: How does "Appearance" in the PAT assess neurological status?**
A: Using the TICLS mnemonic: Tone, Interactiveness, Consolability, Look/Gaze, and Speech/Cry.

**Q3: What is the first step in the primary survey of a critically ill child?**
A: Assessing responsiveness and ensuring safety, followed immediately by assessing Airway with cervical spine protection if trauma is suspected.

**Q4: How do you assess airway patency in an infant versus an older child?**
A: In infants, look for nasal flaring and listen for stridor/grunting; in older children, ask them to speak to assess voice quality and patency.

**Q5: What is the most common cause of airway obstruction in an unconscious child?**
A: Loss of muscle tone causing the tongue to fall back against the posterior pharynx.

**Q6: What is the appropriate head position for opening the airway in an infant?**
A: Neutral or "sniffing" position (avoid hyperextension which can collapse the soft trachea).

**Q7: When is a jaw-thrust maneuver preferred over head-tilt/chin-lift?**
A: When cervical spine injury is suspected.

**Q8: What size oropharyngeal airway (OPA) is appropriate for a child?**
A: Measured from the corner of the mouth to the angle of the mandible.

**Q9: What is the hallmark sign of compensated shock in children?**
A: Tachycardia with maintained blood pressure (hypotension is a late and ominous sign).

**Q10: How is hypotension defined in a child aged 1-10 years?**
A: Systolic BP < 70 + (2 x age in years).

**Q11: What is the initial fluid bolus volume for pediatric resuscitation?**
A: 20 mL/kg of isotonic crystalloid (Normal Saline or Lactated Ringer's).

**Q12: When should you consider switching to blood products during resuscitation?**
A: After 40-60 mL/kg of crystalloid without improvement, or immediately in hemorrhagic shock.

**Q13: What is the normal capillary refill time in a child?**
A: Less than 2 seconds (prolonged >3s indicates poor perfusion).

**Q14: How does respiratory failure typically present in infants compared to adults?**
A: Infants rely on diaphragmatic breathing; abdominal seesaw motion and grunting are early signs of fatigue.

**Q15: What is the significance of grunting in a pediatric patient?**
A: It is an attempt to generate auto-PEEP to keep alveoli open, indicating significant respiratory distress.

**Q16: What heart rate defines bradycardia requiring CPR in an infant?**
A: < 60 bpm despite adequate oxygenation and ventilation.

**Q17: What is the compression-to-ventilation ratio for single-rescuer CPR in a child?**
A: 30:2 (same as adults). For two-rescuer healthcare providers, it is 15:2.

**Q18: Where is the preferred pulse check location for an infant?**
A: Brachial artery.

**Q19: Where is the preferred pulse check location for a child (>1 year)?**
A: Carotid or femoral artery.

**Q20: What is the energy dose for the first defibrillation attempt in a child?**
A: 2 J/kg.

## Section 2: Pediatric Respiratory Emergencies (Questions 21-50)

### Subheading: Upper Airway Obstruction (Croup, Epiglottitis, FB)
**Q21: What is the classic clinical presentation of viral croup (laryngotracheobronchitis)?**
A: Barking cough, inspiratory stridor, hoarseness, often preceded by URI symptoms.

**Q22: What is the most common pathogen causing croup?**
A: Parainfluenza virus types 1 and 2.

**Q23: What radiographic finding is characteristic of croup?**
A: Steeple sign (subglottic narrowing) on AP neck X-ray.

**Q24: What is the first-line pharmacological treatment for moderate-to-severe croup?**
A: Nebulized epinephrine (racemic or L-epinephrine) and systemic corticosteroids (Dexamethasone).

**Q25: How long should a patient be observed after receiving nebulized epinephrine for croup?**
A: At least 2-4 hours to monitor for rebound edema.

**Q26: What is the classic triad of epiglottitis?**
A: Drooling, dysphagia, and distress (tripod positioning). *Note: Rare now due to Hib vaccine.*

**Q27: Why should you avoid examining the throat with a tongue depressor in suspected epiglottitis?**
A: It may precipitate complete laryngospasm and airway obstruction.

**Q28: What is the "thumb sign" on a lateral neck X-ray?**
A: Swollen epiglottis, indicative of epiglottitis.

**Q29: What is the immediate management of a child with suspected foreign body aspiration and severe distress?**
A: Back blows and chest thrusts (infants) or Heimlich maneuver (children >1 year). Do not perform blind finger sweeps.

**Q30: What is the most common site for foreign body lodgment in the airway?**
A: Right main bronchus.

### Subheading: Lower Airway Disease (Bronchiolitis, Asthma, Pneumonia)
**Q31: What is the primary pathogen causing bronchiolitis?**
A: Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV).

**Q32: What is the mainstay of treatment for bronchiolitis?**
A: Supportive care (hydration, nasal suctioning, oxygen if hypoxic). Bronchodilators and steroids are generally NOT recommended.

**Q33: What criteria indicate hospital admission for bronchiolitis?**
A: Hypoxia (<90-92%), inability to feed/dehydration, apnea, or significant comorbidities.

**Q34: What is the definition of status asthmaticus in children?**
A: Severe asthma exacerbation unresponsive to initial standard therapy (SABA + Ipratropium).

**Q35: What is the first-line medication for acute asthma exacerbation?**
A: Short-acting beta-agonists (Albuterol/Salbutamol) via nebulizer or MDI with spacer.

**Q36: When is IV Magnesium Sulfate indicated in pediatric asthma?**
A: In severe exacerbations not responding to inhaled bronchodilators and systemic steroids.

**Q37: What is the dosing for oral Dexamethasone in mild-moderate croup?**
A: 0.15-0.6 mg/kg (usually 0.6 mg/kg for maximal effect), single dose.

**Q38: What clinical score is used to assess croup severity?**
A: Westley Croup Score.

**Q39: What is the role of Heliox in pediatric respiratory distress?**
A: Reduces work of breathing in upper airway obstruction by decreasing turbulence (low density gas).

**Q40: What are the indications for intubation in status asthmaticus?**
A: Impending respiratory arrest, altered mental status, severe fatigue, or worsening hypercapnia despite maximal therapy.

**Q41: What is the typical chest X-ray finding in bacterial pneumonia?**
A: Lobar consolidation.

**Q42: What is the typical chest X-ray finding in viral pneumonia?**
A: Diffuse interstitial infiltrates or hyperinflation.

**Q43: What is the most common bacterial cause of pneumonia in school-aged children?**
A: Streptococcus pneumoniae.

**Q44: What is the most common bacterial cause of pneumonia in neonates?**
A: Group B Streptococcus (GBS) and E. coli.

**Q45: When is empiric coverage for MRSA indicated in pediatric pneumonia?**
A: Severe necrotizing pneumonia, post-influenza, or known colonization.

**Q46: What is the treatment for pertussis (whooping cough)?**
A: Macrolides (Azithromycin) and supportive care; isolation is critical.

**Q47: What is the "steeple sign" absent in, making it a differentiator from croup?**
A: Epiglottitis (which shows thumb sign) or Retropharyngeal abscess.

**Q48: What is a retropharyngeal abscess, and how does it present?**
A: Deep neck space infection presenting with fever, neck stiffness, torticollis, and bulging posterior pharyngeal wall.

**Q49: What imaging is preferred for retropharyngeal abscess?**
A: Lateral neck X-ray (widened prevertebral space) confirmed by CT neck with contrast.

**Q50: What is the management of a peritonsillar abscess (Quinsy)?**
A: Airway protection, IV antibiotics, and needle aspiration or incision & drainage.

## Section 3: Pediatric Cardiac Emergencies (Questions 51-70)

### Subheading: Arrhythmias & Congenital Heart Disease
**Q51: What is the most common cause of tachycardia in children?**
A: Sinus tachycardia secondary to fever, dehydration, pain, or anxiety.

**Q52: How do you differentiate SVT from Sinus Tachycardia on ECG?**
A: SVT has a fixed, very rapid rate (>220 in infants, >180 in children) with no P waves or abnormal P waves; Sinus Tach varies with activity/state and has normal P waves.

**Q53: What is the first-line vagal maneuver for SVT in an infant?**
A: Applying a bag of ice water to the face for 15-30 seconds (diving reflex).

**Q54: What is the first-line drug for stable SVT in children?**
A: Adenosine (0.1 mg/kg rapid push, max 6mg first dose).

**Q55: What is the synchronized cardioversion dose for unstable SVT?**
A: 0.5-1 J/kg initially.

**Q56: What ECG finding suggests Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) syndrome?**
A: Short PR interval and delta wave.

**Q57: Why are AV nodal blocking agents (Digoxin, CCBs) contraindicated in WPW with A-fib?**
A: They can accelerate conduction down the accessory pathway, leading to VF.

**Q58: What is the most common cyanotic congenital heart defect presenting in the newborn period?**
A: Transposition of the Great Arteries (TGA).

**Q59: What is the classic CXR finding in TGA?**
A: "Egg on a string" appearance.

**Q60: What medication is critical to maintain ductal patency in ductal-dependent lesions?**
A: Prostaglandin E1 (Alprostadil).

**Q61: What are the side effects of Prostaglandin E1 infusion?**
A: Apnea, hypotension, fever, flushing.

**Q62: What is the classic murmur of Tetralogy of Fallot?**
A: Harsh systolic ejection murmur at the left upper sternal border (due to RVOT obstruction, not the VSD).

**Q63: What is a "Tet spell" and how is it managed?**
A: Acute cyanotic episode managed by knee-chest position, oxygen, morphine, fluids, and phenylephrine/bicarb.

**Q64: What is the classic CXR finding in Tetralogy of Fallot?**
A: "Boot-shaped" heart (Coeur en sabot).

**Q65: What is the most common cause of heart failure in infants?**
A: Left-to-right shunts (VSD, PDA, AV canal defects).

**Q66: What are the signs of heart failure in an infant during feeding?**
A: Diaphoresis, tachypnea, fatigue, and poor weight gain.

**Q67: What is the management of hypercyanotic spells in TOF?**
A: Calm the child, knee-chest position, 100% O2, morphine, volume expansion, alpha-agonists.

**Q68: What is Long QT Syndrome?**
A: A channelopathy predisposing to Torsades de Pointes and sudden cardiac death.

**Q69: What drugs should be avoided in Long QT Syndrome?**
A: Macrolides, fluoroquinolones, antipsychotics, antiemetics (ondansetron).

**Q70: What is the treatment for Torsades de Pointes?**
A: IV Magnesium Sulfate and defibrillation if unstable.

## Section 4: Pediatric Neurological Emergencies (Questions 71-90)

### Subheading: Seizures & Status Epilepticus
**Q71: What is the definition of status epilepticus in children?**
A: A seizure lasting >5 minutes or recurrent seizures without return to baseline.

**Q72: What is the most common cause of febrile seizures?**
A: Viral infections (HHV-6, Influenza) causing rapid temperature rise.

**Q73: What are the criteria for a "Simple Febrile Seizure"?**
A: Generalized, <15 mins, occurs once in 24 hours, age 6mo-5yrs, neurologically normal baseline.

**Q74: Is a lumbar puncture routinely required for simple febrile seizures?**
A: No, unless there are signs of meningitis or the child is <12 months with incomplete vaccination.

**Q75: What is the first-line benzodiazepine for active seizures in the ED?**
A: Lorazepam (0.1 mg/kg IV) or Midazolam (0.2 mg/kg IM/IN/Buccal).

**Q76: What is the second-line agent for refractory status epilepticus?**
A: Fosphenytoin/Phenytoin (20 mg/kg) or Levetiracetam.

**Q77: What is the third-line therapy for super-refractory status epilepticus?**
A: Continuous infusion of Midazolam, Pentobarbital, or Propofol (intubation required).

**Q78: What metabolic abnormalities commonly cause seizures in neonates?**
A: Hypoglycemia, hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia, hyponatremia.

**Q79: What is the immediate treatment for suspected hypoglycemic seizure?**
A: D10W (2-4 mL/kg) or D25W in older children.

**Q80: What is the concern with Phenytoin infusion in children?**
A: "Purple Glove Syndrome" (tissue necrosis if extravasated) and arrhythmias (give slowly).

### Subheading: Altered Mental Status & Meningitis
**Q81: What is the mnemonic AEIOU-TIPS used for?**
A: Differential diagnosis of Altered Mental Status.

**Q82: What are the classic triad of bacterial meningitis?**
A: Fever, nuchal rigidity, altered mental status (though often incomplete in children).

**Q83: What is the most common cause of bacterial meningitis in unvaccinated children?**
A: Streptococcus pneumoniae and Neisseria meningitidis.

**Q84: What is the empiric antibiotic regimen for neonatal meningitis?**
A: Ampicillin + Gentamicin (or Cefotaxime).

**Q85: What is the empiric antibiotic regimen for children >1 month?**
A: Vancomycin + Ceftriaxone (or Cefotaxime).

**Q86: When should Dexamethasone be given in suspected meningitis?**
A: Before or with the first dose of antibiotics (reduces hearing loss in H. flu and pneumococcal meningitis).

**Q87: What is a contraindication to Lumbar Puncture?**
A: Signs of increased ICP (papilledema, focal deficits, herniation risk), coagulopathy, or overlying skin infection.

**Q88: What is the management of increased intracranial pressure (ICP)?**
A: Head elevation 30°, hyperventilation (temporizing), Mannitol or Hypertonic Saline, sedation.

**Q89: What is the presentation of botulism in infants?**
A: "Floppy baby" syndrome: constipation, weak cry, hypotonia, descending paralysis.

**Q90: What is the treatment for infant botulism?**
A: BabyBIG (Botulism Immune Globulin).

## Section 5: Pediatric Gastrointestinal Emergencies (Questions 91-110)

### Subheading: Abdominal Pain & Obstruction
**Q91: What is the classic presentation of Intussusception?**
A: Intermittent colicky pain, vomiting, "currant jelly" stools, and a sausage-shaped mass in the RUQ.

**Q92: What is the diagnostic and therapeutic test of choice for Intussusception?**
A: Air or contrast enema.

**Q93: What is the classic presentation of Pyloric Stenosis?**
A: Non-bilious projectile vomiting in a 3-6 week old male, hungry after vomiting, olive-shaped mass.

**Q94: What metabolic abnormality is associated with Pyloric Stenosis?**
A: Hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis.

**Q95: What is the definitive treatment for Pyloric Stenosis?**
A: Pyloromyotomy (after correcting fluid/electrolytes).

**Q96: What is Bilious vomiting in a newborn until proven otherwise?**
A: Malrotation with Volvulus.

**Q97: What is the diagnostic test for Malrotation?**
A: Upper GI series (gold standard) showing abnormal position of Ligament of Treitz.

**Q98: What is the surgical emergency associated with Malrotation?**
A: Midgut volvulus (requires immediate surgery to prevent bowel necrosis).

**Q99: What is Meckel's Diverticulum?**
A: Remnant of vitelline duct; can cause painless lower GI bleeding ("currant jelly" or maroon stools) in toddlers.

**Q100: What is the "Rule of 2s" for Meckel's?**
A: 2% of population, 2 feet from ileocecal valve, 2 inches long, 2 types of ectopic tissue, presents before age 2.

**Q101: What is Appendicitis presentation in young children?**
A: Often atypical: vomiting before pain, diffuse pain, high fever, diarrhea.

**Q102: What is the most common cause of lower GI bleed in toddlers?**
A: Meckel's diverticulum.

**Q103: What is Necrotizing Enterocolitis (NEC)?**
A: Life-threatening GI disease in premature infants; signs include feeding intolerance, bloody stools, pneumatosis intestinalis.

**Q104: What is the management of NEC?**
A: Bowel rest, NG decompression, broad-spectrum antibiotics, surgery if perforation.

**Q105: What is Hirschsprung Disease?**
A: Congenital aganglionosis of the colon causing failure to pass meconium in first 48h and chronic constipation.

**Q106: What is the diagnostic test for Hirschsprung?**
A: Rectal biopsy showing absence of ganglion cells.

**Q107: What is the presentation of Testicular Torsion?**
A: Acute scrotal pain, high-riding testis, absent cremasteric reflex.

**Q108: What is the time window for salvaging a torsed testicle?**
A: <6 hours (near 100% salvage); >24 hours (near 0%).

**Q109: What is the differential for acute scrotum?**
A: Torsion, epididymitis, torsion of appendix testis, trauma, incarcerated hernia.

**Q110: What is the "Blue Dot Sign"?**
A: Visible ischemic appendix testis, pathognomonic for torsion of the appendix testis (benign).

## Section 6: Pediatric Infectious Emergencies (Questions 111-130)

### Subheading: Sepsis & Fever Without Source
**Q111: What defines Pediatric Sepsis?**
A: Suspected/proven infection + Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (SIRS) + Organ Dysfunction.

**Q112: What are the SIRS criteria for a child?**
A: Temp abnormality, HR abnormality (age-specific), RR abnormality, WBC count abnormality.

**Q113: What is the "Golden Hour" bundle for pediatric septic shock?**
A: Fluid boluses (up to 60ml/kg), early antibiotics (within 1 hour), vasoactive support if fluid refractory.

**Q114: What is Fever Without Source (FWS) in a neonate (<28 days)?**
A: Temp >38.0°C; requires full septic workup (CBC, Blood Cx, Urine Cx, LP) and admission for IV antibiotics.

**Q115: What is the Rochester Criteria used for?**
A: Identifying low-risk febrile infants (29-90 days) who may not need full workup/admission.

**Q116: What is the most common serious bacterial infection in febrile infants?**
A: Urinary Tract Infection (UTI).

**Q117: How should urine be collected in a non-toilet trained child for culture?**
A: Catheterization or Suprapubic aspiration (bag specimens are for screening only).

**Q118: What is Kawasaki Disease?**
A: Vasculitis of medium vessels; fever >5 days + 4/5 criteria (Conjunctivitis, Rash, Adenopathy, Strawberry tongue, Extremity changes).

**Q119: What is the most feared complication of Kawasaki Disease?**
A: Coronary artery aneurysms.

**Q120: What is the treatment for Kawasaki Disease?**
A: IVIG (2g/kg) + High dose Aspirin.

**Q121: What is Meningococcemia?**
A: Invasive N. meningitidis infection; presents with petechial/purpuric rash and rapid shock.

**Q122: What is the immediate action for suspected Meningococcemia?**
A: Isolation, immediate antibiotics (Ceftriaxone), and supportive care.

**Q123: What is Toxic Shock Syndrome (TSS)?**
A: Toxin-mediated disease (Staph/Strep) causing fever, rash (desquamating), hypotension, multi-organ failure.

**Q124: What is the difference between Staph and Strep TSS?**
A: Staph TSS often associated with tampons/wounds, lower mortality; Strep TSS often associated with soft tissue infection/necrotizing fasciitis, higher mortality.

**Q125: What is Herpetic Gingivostomatitis?**
A: Primary HSV-1 infection; painful oral ulcers, fever, drooling, refusal to eat.

**Q126: What is the treatment for severe Herpetic Gingivostomatitis?**
A: Acyclovir (if early) and pain control/hydration.

**Q127: What is Hand-Foot-and-Mouth Disease?**
A: Coxsackievirus; vesicular rash on hands, feet, and mouth.

**Q128: What is Roseola Infantum?**
A: HHV-6; high fever for 3-5 days followed by rash as fever resolves.

**Q129: What is Erythema Infectiosum (Fifth Disease)?**
A: Parvovirus B19; "Slapped cheek" rash.

**Q130: What is the risk of Parvovirus B19 in pregnancy?**
A: Hydrops fetalis and fetal loss.

## Section 7: Pediatric Trauma & Non-Accidental Injury (Questions 131-150)

### Subheading: Trauma Assessment & Specific Injuries
**Q131: What is the leading cause of death in children?**
A: Trauma (specifically blunt trauma from MVCs).

**Q132: How does pediatric anatomy affect trauma patterns?**
A: Larger head-to-body ratio (head injuries), flexible ribs (pulmonary contusion without fracture), solid organs closer together (multi-organ injury).

**Q133: What is the primary mechanism of spinal cord injury without radiographic abnormality (SCIWORA)?**
A: Hyperflexion/extension in children <8 years due to ligamentous laxity.

**Q134: What is the most common solid organ injury in pediatric trauma?**
A: Spleen.

**Q135: What is the management of hemodynamically stable splenic/liver injury?**
A: Non-operative management (observation, bed rest).

**Q136: When is CT scanning indicated in pediatric head trauma?**
A: Based on PECARN rules (GCS <15, signs of basilar skull fx, severe mechanism, vomiting, altered behavior).

**Q137: What are signs of Basilar Skull Fracture?**
A: Battle sign, Raccoon eyes, Hemotympanum, CSF rhinorrhea/otorrhea.

**Q138: What is Shaken Baby Syndrome (Abusive Head Trauma)?**
A: Triad of Encephalopathy, Subdural Hematoma, and Retinal Hemorrhages.

**Q139: What fractures are highly specific for Non-Accidental Trauma (NAT)?**
A: Posterior rib fractures, metaphyseal corner fractures (bucket handle), spiral fractures in non-ambulatory children.

**Q140: What is the skeletal survey?**
A: Full body X-ray series to detect occult fractures in suspected NAT (<2 years old).

**Q141: When should you suspect NAT?**
A: History inconsistent with injury, delay in seeking care, multiple injuries in various stages of healing.

**Q142: What is the mandatory reporting requirement for physicians regarding NAT?**
A: Immediate report to Child Protective Services (CPS) upon suspicion (proof not required).

**Q143: What is a Toddler's Fracture?**
A: Spiral tibial fracture from minor twist; often benign but must distinguish from abuse.

**Q144: What is the most common cause of fatal child abuse?**
A: Head trauma.

**Q145: What retinal finding is pathognomonic for abusive head trauma?**
A: Multilayered retinal hemorrhages extending to the periphery.

**Q146: What is the "seatbelt sign"?**
A: Bruising across the abdomen/chest from a lap belt; associated with bowel perforation and lumbar spine fractures (Chance fracture).

**Q147: What is the concern with handlebar injuries to the abdomen?**
A: Pancreatic or duodenal trauma (blunt force to epigastrium).

**Q148: What is the initial fluid resuscitation for pediatric trauma?**
A: 20 mL/kg bolus, repeat up to 60 mL/kg, then blood.

**Q149: What is the ATLS classification for pediatric hemorrhagic shock?**
A: Similar to adults but relies more on HR and mental status than BP.

**Q150: What is the role of FAST exam in pediatric trauma?**
A: Rapid detection of free fluid; less sensitive in children than adults but useful for triage.

## Section 8: Pediatric Toxicology & Environmental (Questions 151-170)

### Subheading: Common Ingestions & Poisonings
**Q151: What is the most common cause of fatal poisoning in children <6 years?**
A: Opioids (increasingly) and Carbon Monoxide; historically lead and iron.

**Q152: What is the universal antidote for opioid overdose?**
A: Naloxone.

**Q153: What is the toxic dose of Acetaminophen?**
A: >150 mg/kg (acute).

**Q154: What is the antidote for Acetaminophen toxicity?**
A: N-acetylcysteine (NAC).

**Q155: When is the Rumack-Matthew nomogram used?**
A: For acute single ingestion of acetaminophen between 4-24 hours.

**Q156: What are the stages of Iron toxicity?**
A: 1: GI hemorrhage/vomiting; 2: Latent; 3: Shock/Metabolic acidosis; 4: Hepatic failure; 5: Stricture.

**Q157: What is the antidote for Iron toxicity?**
A: Deferoxamine.

**Q158: What is the classic presentation of Salicylate toxicity?**
A: Vomiting, tinnitus, hyperventilation (resp alkalosis + metabolic acidosis), hyperthermia.

**Q159: What is the treatment for Salicylate toxicity?**
A: Urine alkalinization (Bicarb), glucose, dialysis if severe.

**Q160: What is the toxic exposure risk from Button Batteries?**
A: Esophageal impaction causing liquefactive necrosis and perforation within hours.

**Q161: What is the immediate management of esophageal button battery?**
A: Emergent endoscopic removal. Give honey/sucralfate if delay >2hrs (if >1yr old).

**Q162: What is the danger of high-powered magnets ingestion?**
A: Multiple magnets can trap bowel loops between them causing ischemia/perforation.

**Q163: What is the management of Caustic Ingestion (lye/acid)?**
A: Airway protection, NPO, nothing by mouth, no emesis, early endoscopy to grade injury.

**Q164: What is Lead Poisoning presentation?**
A: Chronic: developmental delay, anemia, abdominal pain. Acute: encephalopathy.

**Q165: What is the treatment for severe Lead Poisoning?**
A: Dimercaprol + EDTA or Succimer.

**Q166: What is Carbon Monoxide (CO) poisoning presentation?**
A: Headache, nausea, confusion, "cherry red" skin (rare).

**Q167: What is the treatment for CO poisoning?**
A: 100% Oxygen; Hyperbaric oxygen for pregnant women, loss of consciousness, or severe acidosis.

**Q168: What is the risk of Benzocaine spray in children?**
A: Methemoglobinemia.

**Q169: What is the treatment for Methemoglobinemia?**
A: Methylene Blue (contraindicated in G6PD deficiency).

**Q170: What is the "one pill kills" rule for toddlers?**
A: Applies to CCBs, Beta-blockers, Sulfonylureas, Opioids, TCAs, Salicylates, Camphor, Iron.

## Section 9: Pediatric Dermatology & Rheumatology (Questions 171-185)

**Q171: What is Erythema Multiforme?**
A: Target lesions, often post-HSV; self-limiting.

**Q172: What is Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)?**
A: Severe mucocutaneous reaction (<10% BSA), usually drug-induced.

**Q173: What is Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN)?**
A: Severe variant of SJS (>30% BSA detachment), high mortality.

**Q174: What is the management of SJS/TEN?**
A: Stop offending drug, supportive care (burn unit), IVIG/Cyclosporine (controversial).

**Q175: What is Henoch-Schonlein Purpura (IgA Vasculitis)?**
A: Palpable purpura on buttocks/legs, abdominal pain, arthritis, renal involvement.

**Q176: What is the treatment for HSP?**
A: Supportive; steroids for severe abdominal pain or renal involvement.

**Q177: What is Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)?**
A: Chronic arthritis >6 weeks in child <16yrs.

**Q178: What is the emergency presentation of JIA?**
A: Uveitis (silent vision loss) or Macrophage Activation Syndrome (MAS).

**Q179: What is Impetigo?**
A: Honey-crusted lesions, Staph/Strep; treat with topical Mupirocin or oral antibiotics.

**Q180: What is Cellulitis vs Abscess?**
A: Cellulitis = diffuse erythema (treat abx); Abscess = fluctuant collection (treat I&D).

**Q181: What is Scalded Skin Syndrome?**
A: Staph exotoxin causing widespread desquamation; Nikolsky sign positive.

**Q182: What is the difference between SSSS and TEN?**
A: SSSS spares mucosa, affects children; TEN involves mucosa, affects adults/drugs.

**Q183: What is Urticaria?**
A: Hives; allergic reaction. Treat with antihistamines, epi if anaphylaxis.

**Q184: What is Angioedema?**
A: Deep tissue swelling; can compromise airway.

**Q185: What is the genetic form of angioedema?**
A: Hereditary Angioedema (C1 esterase inhibitor deficiency); treat with C1 inhibitor concentrate or Icatibant.

## Section 10: Special Situations & Procedures (Questions 186-200)

### Subheading: Procedures & Ethics
**Q186: What is the IO (Intraosseous) access indication?**
A: Vascular access failed or impossible in cardiac arrest/shock.

**Q187: Where is the preferred IO site in children?**
A: Proximal tibia (flat medial surface).

**Q188: What fluids can be given via IO?**
A: Any IV fluid, blood, medications (same doses as IV).

**Q189: What is the complication of IO placement?**
A: Extravasation, compartment syndrome, fracture, osteomyelitis.

**Q190: What is the reduction maneuver for Nursemaid's Elbow?**
A: Hyperpronation or Supination/Flexion of the forearm.

**Q191: What is the clinical sign of successful Nursemaid's reduction?**
A: Child uses arm normally within minutes.

**Q192: What is the approach to a crying child needing a procedure?**
A: Topical anesthetics (LET/EMLA), distraction, sucrose (infants), parental presence.

**Q193: What is the legal concept of "Emancipated Minor"?**
A: Minors who can consent for themselves (married, military, parent, living independently).

**Q194: Can minors consent for STI/Pregnancy/Drug treatment?**
A: Yes, in most jurisdictions without parental consent.

**Q195: When can you treat a minor without parental consent in an emergency?**
A: Doctrine of implied consent for life/limb-threatening emergencies.

**Q196: What is the approach to a refusing parent for life-saving treatment?**
A: Court order; treat in immediate life threat regardless.

**Q197: What is the definition of Apparent Life-Threatening Event (ALTE) / BRUE?**
A: Brief Resolved Unexplained Event: color change, tone change, apnea, choking in infant <1yr.

**Q198: What is the workup for Low Risk BRUE?**
A: Often none; observation and discharge with follow-up.

**Q199: What is SIDS?**
A: Sudden Infant Death Syndrome; diagnosis of exclusion after autopsy.

**Q200: What is the primary prevention for SIDS?**
A: Back to sleep, firm mattress, no soft bedding, room sharing without bed sharing.
