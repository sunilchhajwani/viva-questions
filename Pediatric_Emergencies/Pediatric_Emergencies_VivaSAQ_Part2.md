# Master Case: Pediatric Emergencies (Continued)

## Section 6: Pediatric Gastrointestinal Emergencies

**Q51: What is the classic presentation of intussusception?**
A: Intermittent colicky abdominal pain, vomiting, "currant jelly" stools, sausage-shaped mass.

**Q52: What age group gets intussusception?**
A: Most common 3 months to 3 years (peak 5-9 months).

**Q53: How do you diagnose intussusception?**
A: Ultrasound showing "target sign" or "doughnut sign."

**Q54: How do you treat intussusception?**
A: Air or contrast enema (diagnostic and therapeutic), surgery if failed or peritonitis.

**Q55: What is the classic presentation of pyloric stenosis?**
A: 2-6 week old, projectile non-bilious vomiting, hungry after vomiting, olive-shaped mass.

**Q56: What metabolic abnormality in pyloric stenosis?**
A: Hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis.

**Q57: How do you diagnose pyloric stenosis?**
A: Ultrasound showing thickened pylorus (>3mm) and elongated channel (>14mm).

**Q58: What is bilious vomiting in a newborn until proven otherwise?**
A: Midgut volvulus (surgical emergency).

**Q59: What imaging for suspected volvulus?**
A: Upper GI series showing "corkscrew" appearance or abnormal ligament of Treitz.

**Q60: What is the management of midgut volvulus?**
A: Immediate surgical consultation, NG decompression, IV fluids, emergent laparotomy.

## Section 7: Pediatric Infectious Emergencies

**Q61: What age requires full septic workup for fever?**
A: Neonates <28 days (some protocols 0-60 days) - CBC, blood culture, urine culture, LP.

**Q62: What empiric antibiotics for neonatal sepsis?**
A: Ampicillin + gentamicin (or cefotaxime).

**Q63: What organisms cause neonatal sepsis?**
A: Group B Strep, E. coli, Listeria monocytogenes.

**Q64: What is the Rochester criteria?**
A: Low-risk criteria for febrile infants 0-60 days to avoid admission/LP.

**Q65: What is Kawasaki disease diagnostic criteria?**
A: Fever >5 days + 4/5: conjunctivitis, rash, adenopathy, strawberry tongue, extremity changes.

**Q66: How do you treat Kawasaki disease?**
A: IVIG 2 g/kg single dose + aspirin 80-100 mg/kg/day (anti-inflammatory dose).

**Q67: What is the concern with Kawasaki disease?**
A: Coronary artery aneurysms (echo monitoring required).

**Q68: What is meningococcemia presentation?**
A: Fever, petechial/purpuric rash, rapid progression to shock, DIC.

**Q69: How do you treat suspected meningococcemia?**
A: Ceftriaxone or penicillin G, droplet precautions, chemoprophylaxis for contacts.

**Q70: What is the treatment for pertussis?**
A: Azithromycin 10 mg/kg day 1, then 5 mg/kg days 2-5 (macrolides).

## Section 8: Pediatric Trauma

**Q71: What is the most common cause of death in children?**
A: Trauma (blunt trauma most common).

**Q72: What are unique pediatric trauma considerations?**
A: Larger head, flexible ribs (pulmonary contusion without fracture), smaller blood volume, hypothermia risk.

**Q73: What fluid bolus for hemorrhagic shock?**
A: 20 mL/kg crystalloid, if no response → 10 mL/kg PRBC.

**Q74: What is the total blood volume in children?**
A: ~80 mL/kg (neonates 85-90 mL/kg).

**Q75: When do you worry about non-accidental trauma (NAT)?**
A: Inconsistent history, developmental stage mismatch, multiple injuries, delay in seeking care.

**Q76: What fractures are highly specific for NAT?**
A: Posterior rib fractures, metaphyseal corner fractures, scapular fractures, spinous process fractures.

**Q77: What screening for NAT?**
A: Skeletal survey (<2yr), CT head, ophthalmology exam (retinal hemorrhages), coagulation studies.

**Q78: What is shaken baby syndrome triad?**
A: Subdural hematoma, retinal hemorrhages, encephalopathy.

**Q79: What is the most common intra-abdominal injury in children?**
A: Splenic injury (blunt trauma).

**Q80: How do you manage solid organ injury in stable children?**
A: Non-operative management (bed rest, serial exams, hemoglobin monitoring).

## Section 9: Pediatric Toxicology

**Q81: What is the most common pediatric poisoning?**
A: Acetaminophen, followed by household cleaners, cosmetics, plants.

**Q82: What is the antidote for acetaminophen toxicity?**
A: N-acetylcysteine (NAC) - oral or IV protocol.

**Q83: When do you use the Rumack-Matthew nomogram?**
A: Single acute ingestion, level drawn 4+ hours post-ingestion.

**Q84: What is the toxic dose of acetaminophen?**
A: >150 mg/kg acute ingestion (or >75 mg/kg with risk factors).

**Q85: What medications cause dangerous toxicity in small ingestions for children?**
A: Calcium channel blockers, beta-blockers, clonidine, sulfonylureas, opioids, TCAs.

**Q86: What is the presentation of clonidine ingestion?**
A: Bradycardia, hypotension, miosis, CNS depression (mimics opioid overdose).

**Q87: How do you treat sulfonylurea-induced hypoglycemia?**
A: Octreotide 1-2 mcg/kg SQ/IV, dextrose bolus, continuous infusion, admit for observation.

**Q88: What is the danger of button battery ingestion?**
A: Esophageal impaction causes liquefactive necrosis within 2 hours (emergency removal).

**Q89: What is the management of magnet ingestion?**
A: Multiple magnets or magnet + metal object = emergency (risk of bowel perforation).

**Q90: Do you induce vomiting in pediatric ingestions?**
A: NO - ipecac contraindicated, activated charcoal rarely used in ED.

## Section 10: Pediatric Environmental Emergencies

**Q91: How do you define fever in children?**
A: Temperature ≥38.0°C (100.4°F).

**Q92: When is fever an emergency?**
A: Age <28 days, immunocompromised, toxic appearance, signs of serious bacterial infection.

**Q93: What is heat stroke definition?**
A: Core temperature >40°C with CNS dysfunction (confusion, seizure, coma).

**Q94: How do you treat heat stroke?**
A: Rapid cooling (ice water immersion best), IV fluids, monitor for complications.

**Q95: Why avoid antipyretics in heat stroke?**
A: Hyperthermia is not mediated by prostaglandins (unlike fever).

**Q96: What is the concern with drowning in children?**
A: Hypoxic brain injury, pulmonary edema, hypothermia.

**Q97: Can you perform Heimlich on infants?**
A: NO - use 5 back blows followed by 5 chest thrusts for choking infant.

**Q98: What is the energy for synchronized cardioversion in unstable tachycardia?**
A: 0.5-1 J/kg initially, then 2 J/kg.

**Q99: What is the normal urine output for children?**
A: 1-2 mL/kg/hr (infants), 0.5-1 mL/kg/hr (older children).

**Q100: What is the Parkland formula for pediatric burns?**
A: 4 mL x kg x %TBSA (half in first 8hrs), plus maintenance fluids.
